The Examined Life: Why Every Leader Needs a Year-End Review

Socrates famously declared that the unexamined life is not worth living. Doug Smith, host of The L3 Leadership Podcast, has built that philosophical imperative into an annual ritual — one he has practiced every year since 2004, and one he believes is among the highest-leverage habits a leader can develop. The year-end review is not productivity theater. It is a structured confrontation with reality: what happened, what it meant, and what you need to become because of it.

The premise is deceptively simple. You don't grow by getting another year older. You grow by reflecting, learning, and making changes as necessary. John Maxwell captured the logic precisely: Experience isn't the best teacher. Evaluated experience is. The year-end review is nothing more — and nothing less — than a disciplined process for evaluating the year that just passed.

Smith has been refining his process for over two decades, learning from mentors, studying how other leaders reflect, and iterating year after year. What he's built is comprehensive enough to feel daunting at first glance, but he's clear that the goal is not to copy his system. The best year-end review process is the one you'll actually follow. The point is to build your own.

Why Bother: Three Arguments for the Practice

Before explaining how to do a year-end review, Smith makes the case for why — and the reasons are more layered than simple self-improvement.

The first argument is the growth argument. Most people mistake longevity for development. They assume that living through another year automatically deposits wisdom. It doesn't. Unexamined experience produces repetition, not growth. The year-end review breaks that cycle by forcing a deliberate audit: what actually happened, what patterns emerged, what worked and what didn't, and what changes are now required.

The second argument is the documentation argument. Imagine being able to return to any year of your life and read a precise summary — the goals you held, the losses you absorbed, the lessons you extracted. Smith takes this further: he does decade reviews, pulling together all his annual reviews when a new decade begins. When he turned 30, he assembled his twenties into a single narrative arc. The result, he says, was extraordinary. You could see the shape of a life — the patterns that only become visible at scale, the growth that's invisible year-to-year but unmistakable across ten years.

There's a third argument he raises almost as an afterthought, but it's worth sitting with: a completed set of year-end reviews is one of the most unusual gifts you could leave your children. Not a financial inheritance, but an intellectual and emotional one — a record of how you thought, what you valued, what you struggled with, and what you learned. Whether or not your kids care when you hand it to them at eighty, the archive exists. The life is documented.

Before You Begin: Setting Up for Success

A year-end review that stays theoretical is worthless. Smith's first practical instruction is blunt: schedule it. Block time during the last week of the year and treat it with the same seriousness you'd treat a board meeting or a critical client conversation. The format is flexible — a few focused hours in the morning, a full day, or even multiple days — but the commitment is not optional.

Location matters more than most people think. The goal is a space where you won't be distracted and where you can actually think. For some, that's a home office with the phone off. For others, it requires more friction — a cabin, a hotel room, somewhere that signals to your brain that this time is different. Whatever it takes to get quiet enough to reflect honestly.

Smith assembles a specific set of materials before he begins, and the list reveals something about how he thinks about the process. He brings:

  • His journal — the primary raw material for the review. If you don't journal yet, Smith recommends starting immediately, and suggests the Five Minute Journal app as a low-friction entry point that takes only two to three minutes daily.
  • His calendar — a chronological record of how time was actually spent, not how it was intended to be spent.
  • Photos from the year — a suggestion he credits to Matt Keller. Photos surface memories that written records miss, and they capture emotional texture that calendars and journals can't.
  • His life plan — a document built from Michael Hyatt's Living Forward framework, which defines what he's building across every dimension of his life. Without a life plan, there's no north star against which to evaluate whether the year moved in the right direction.
  • His goals — written out and tracked throughout the year. Smith writes his goals weekly, so by year-end he has a clear picture of both intention and execution.
  • Financial records — he uses Mint to generate a clear picture of how money was earned, spent, and saved.
  • His bucket list — not just to check items off, but to add new ones. The bucket list is a running inventory of ambition; the year-end review is a natural moment to expand it.

The materials aren't bureaucratic overhead. They're the evidence base. Without them, the review degenerates into vague impressions. With them, you're working from data.

The Report: What to Actually Capture

At the center of Smith's process is a written report — a comprehensive document that becomes the permanent record of the year. He walks through each section methodically, and the scope is ambitious. For a first-year reviewer, some sections will be thin. That's fine. Do it anyway, and build the habit of capturing throughout the year so future reviews have richer material to work with.

Highlights and Lowlights. Start with the obvious poles: the peaks and the valleys. What were the best moments of the year? What were the losses, the disappointments, the things that didn't go as planned? Both matter. The lowlights often contain more learning than the highlights.

Themes. Step back from events and ask what overarching themes defined the year. For Smith, this is often a spiritual category — what was God emphasizing in his life this year? For a secular practitioner, the question still applies: what kept coming up? What did the year seem to be about?

Lessons Learned. Smith calls this the biggest section in his report, and it makes sense. Everything else in the review is raw material; lessons learned is the synthesis. What do you now know that you didn't know at the start of the year? What changed your thinking? What experience taught you something that a book couldn't have?

Memories. Distinct from highlights, this is a comprehensive catalog of the year's memorable moments — funny stories, unexpected encounters, small victories. The goal is to make the year retrievable as experience, not just data.

How I Spent My Time. This section requires honesty. Smith looks at his calendar and asks whether his time allocation matches his stated priorities. Were there too many breakfast meetings? Not enough time at home? The calendar doesn't lie about where your attention actually went.

Tough Questions I Was Asked. Smith actively seeks feedback from leaders throughout the year, and those conversations often surface hard questions he needs to sit with. Keeping a record of them ensures they don't get lost. It's a way of holding yourself accountable to the discomfort of being challenged.

Areas to Grow. Once the lessons start accumulating, patterns emerge about where growth is most needed. This section turns lessons into a development agenda for the coming year.

Action Steps I Didn't Follow Through On. Honest self-assessment requires tracking not just what you did, but what you said you'd do and didn't. This section prevents the convenient amnesia that lets unfinished commitments quietly disappear.

Encouragement. What did someone say to you this year that genuinely fired you up? What affirmations, recognitions, or words of belief do you need documented so you can return to them on difficult days? Smith keeps this section precisely because leadership can be isolating, and having a record of real encouragement matters when the hard stretches come.

Prayers and Answered Prayers. For Smith, this is a faith practice — tracking what he brought to God in prayer and where he saw those prayers answered. The principle transfers to any reflective framework: what did you ask for or work toward? What actually came through?

Opportunities. What doors opened this year that you didn't expect? What possibilities emerged that weren't visible at the start of the year? Documenting opportunities builds a pattern of recognizing them.

Accomplishments. Separate from goals — this is about what you're actually proud of. Achievements worth claiming explicitly, not just logging.

Most Influential People. Smith identifies the top three people who most shaped his year. This section connects directly to one of his most powerful post-review practices.

Goal Assessment. How did each goal perform? Which ones were hit, which were missed, and why? The "why" matters as much as the outcome — missed goals often reveal faulty assumptions, unrealistic timelines, or misaligned priorities.

Stats. Smith tracks hard numbers: speaking engagements, podcast downloads per episode, social media metrics, website analytics. The specific stats will vary by person and profession, but the practice of tracking quantitative data creates accountability and surfaces trends that qualitative reflection can miss.

Books Read. A log of the year's reading, which can also become a record of intellectual development over time.

He also includes lighter sections — best movies, funniest YouTube videos, places traveled, bucket list items completed — because a year is a full human experience, not just a professional performance review. The lighter sections keep the document honest about what life actually contains.

After the Review: What to Do With What You've Built

The report is not an end in itself. Smith has a specific set of practices for what happens after the review is complete, and they're worth treating as seriously as the review itself.

First: save it in multiple places. Hard drive, cloud storage, printed copy in a physical folder. You're building an archive. Treat it like one.

Second: plan the year ahead. The year-end review and the year-beginning planning session are natural complements. The review tells you where you are and what you've learned. The planning session sets the direction for what comes next. Smith does both in sequence.

Third — and this is the practice Smith highlights with the most enthusiasm: write handwritten letters to the top three most influential people in your life that year. Let them know specifically what they meant to you. Deliver the letters in person if possible; mail them if not. The impact of receiving a handwritten letter telling you that you were one of the three most influential people in someone's year is difficult to overstate. It's a practice that costs almost nothing and returns enormous relational capital.

More broadly, he reaches out to everyone who made an impact — texting or emailing people to thank them specifically for what they contributed. The year-end review surfaces these moments; the follow-through honors them.

For those with spouses, Smith advocates involving them directly. His wife Laura completes her own year-end review, and they go out for a New Year's dinner to share what they each learned, what their highlights were, and what they're carrying into the next year. A year-end dinner with a partner is one of the highest-leverage relationship rituals Smith describes — a structured conversation that deepens understanding of each other's inner life.

Finally, Smith publishes his lessons. Each year he writes a post or records an episode sharing the top things he learned, because teaching is the most powerful form of consolidation. If you want something to stick, find a way to transmit it.

The Compound Interest of Reflection

The year-end review is a practice that improves with repetition. The first one will be thin — if you haven't been journaling or capturing throughout the year, the raw material will be sparse. Do it anyway. The act of building the habit matters more than the quality of the first output.

What Smith is describing is, at its core, the application of compound interest to self-knowledge. Each review builds on the previous ones. Each year's lessons inform the next year's choices. Each decade review reveals patterns that no single year could surface. The person who has done this for twenty years doesn't just know more than the person who hasn't. They have a fundamentally different relationship with their own experience — one characterized by intentionality, continuity, and the hard-won capacity to actually learn from what they've lived through.

The unexamined life is not worth living. The year-end review is the practice of examination made concrete.

What To Do With This

  • Schedule your year-end review before you finish reading this. Block time during the last week of the year, identify a distraction-free location, and treat it as a non-negotiable appointment. The review that doesn't get scheduled doesn't happen.
  • Download the Five Minute Journal app and start using it daily. Two to three minutes per day builds the raw material that makes future year-end reviews richer. Even a partial year of journaling gives you more to work with than none.
  • Assemble your materials before you sit down. Pull together your journal, calendar, photos, life plan, goals, financial records, and bucket list ahead of time. Arriving prepared removes friction and keeps the review session focused on reflection rather than retrieval.
  • Build the "Lessons Learned" section first and most completely. This is the highest-value section of the review — it synthesizes everything else. If you only have time for one section, make it this one.
  • Create an "Action Steps I Didn't Follow Through On" section. This is the hardest section to complete honestly and the most valuable. Tracking your own follow-through failures prevents the pattern from repeating silently.
  • Track at least three quantitative stats that matter to your work. Decide what numbers reflect progress in your professional life — output, reach, revenue, whatever is relevant — and document them annually. Numbers create accountability that impressions can't.
  • Write handwritten letters to the top three people who shaped your year. Don't email. Don't text. Write by hand, and either deliver in person or mail. This is the single highest-return post-review action Smith describes.
  • If you're married or partnered, do a joint year-end dinner. Have your partner complete their own review independently, then share highlights and lessons over a dedicated dinner. The conversation that results is qualitatively different from ordinary end-of-year talk.
  • Save your report in at least three places. You're building an archive. Cloud storage, local drive, and a printed copy. A decade from now, you'll be glad the document still exists.
  • Read Living Forward by Michael Hyatt and build a life plan. A year-end review without a life plan lacks a north star. The review tells you what happened; the life plan tells you whether what happened moved you in the right direction.
