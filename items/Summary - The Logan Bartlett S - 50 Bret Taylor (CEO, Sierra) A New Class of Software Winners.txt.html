The $20 Phone Call That Changes Everything

Bret Taylor has a thought experiment he uses to explain why AI agents aren't just a cost-cutting story. Imagine you run a telecom company. Your entire business hinges on subscriber lifetime value — who upgrades to a higher tier, who churns when a competitor runs a promotion. Today, a phone call with a customer costs you $20. With AI agents, that same conversation costs twenty cents. The obvious move is to bank the operating expense savings. But that's the wrong frame entirely.

"When the price of a phone call starts to approach the price of a page view," Taylor says, "you're going to do a lot more of them." At twenty cents a conversation, you don't just automate the same volume of calls — you rethink the entire relationship. You proactively reach out to subscribers before they see a competitor's ad. You upsell at moments of high intent. You turn what was a cost center into a revenue engine. The unit economics of customer engagement just got inverted, and most companies haven't started working through what that means.

This is the core of how Taylor thinks about Sierra, the company he co-founded with Clay Bavor to build AI agents for customer experience. The firm recently closed a major financing round, and Taylor came on the Logan Bartlett Show to think through the landscape — where the AI wave parallels the original internet era, where the analogy breaks down, and what it actually takes to win in a market where the obvious opportunities are obvious to everyone.

AltaVista Was First Too

The parallel Taylor reaches for most often is the dot-com era, and not in a cautionary way. He sees genuine structural similarities. In both cases, the biggest markets were legible from the start. "You don't need to be that savvy about the internet to think, wow, it might be useful if people could buy things digitally," he observes. Search, e-commerce, payments — the concept was obvious. The question was always execution: who gets the product right, who picks the right go-to-market model, who builds the right business model for the moment.

He uses Inktomi as a sharper example than AltaVista. Inktomi had strong engineering; Taylor argues PageRank was better, but it wasn't a blowout. What killed Inktomi was a B2B model — they licensed their search engine to portals, which meant they never had the chance to build AdWords, "which turned out to be the greatest business model of all time." Google was first in the right direction; Inktomi was first in the wrong direction.

The AI equivalent is playing out in real time. Software engineering, customer service, content, legal, visual effects — these are the obvious categories, analogous to search and e-commerce in 1997. As a result, one of Sierra's official company values is "competitive intensity." The first line of that value, Taylor notes, is: "We know we're not entitled to our success." It's an unusual thing to codify, but in a market where the opportunity is visible to everyone, humility about that entitlement is actually the realistic stance.

The New TAM: When Agents Do the Work

One place where the AI wave genuinely diverges from prior platform shifts is in how it redraws the map of addressable markets. Taylor points to Harvey — an AI company focused on legal work — as his favorite illustration. Legal tech has never produced a top-ten enterprise software company. The TAM for selling productivity tools to lawyers simply wasn't that large. Law firms are small businesses that don't buy much software per lawyer.

But Harvey isn't selling productivity enhancement. Harvey is doing the antitrust review. "If you're actually doing the work," Taylor says, "all of a sudden the total addressable market looks huge because the addressable market of legal advice and legal labor is actually quite large." The shift from "tools that help humans work faster" to "agents that do the work" unlocks sectors that traditional enterprise software never penetrated — not because the software wasn't good enough, but because the labor market it was addressing was never the software's to capture.

This reframes how you value an AI agent entirely. A Sierra agent that completes a sale doesn't get valued on software productivity metrics. It gets valued on the margin of that sale, or on the commission you would have paid a human to make it. "The way you would value it is not really even related to AI or software," Taylor says. That's not a subtle distinction — it's a categorical shift in how software companies justify their existence and price their products.

From Impressions to Clicks: The Outcome-Based Pricing Argument

When Logan Bartlett asks whether competition will eventually compress outcome-based pricing the way it compressed per-seat pricing for Zoom or Slack, Taylor offers an analogy that reframes the question. Moving from seat-based to outcome-based pricing isn't leaving money on the table — it's moving from impression-based to cost-per-click advertising. "That would be like making a modern cost-per-click ads auction and saying you're leaving impressions on the table," he says. No one in digital advertising thinks that way anymore because history has settled the question.

The reason horizontal productivity tools like Zoom end up commoditized, Taylor argues, is that the value they deliver is genuinely hard to measure. You pay the same per-seat price for your most senior engineer and your newest hire, regardless of how much they rely on the product. There's no clean line from "this video call happened" to "this business outcome occurred." In contrast, vertical enterprise software — ServiceNow for ITSM, Salesforce for CRM, SAP for ERP — has always commanded dramatically higher per-seat pricing precisely because it sits closer to measurable business value.

Outcome-based pricing takes that logic to its endpoint. In Sierra's case, companies already know their cost per contact in their call centers. They already know what they pay a salesperson to close a deal. When an AI agent does that work, the value is legible, and the pricing can reflect it. "For companies, it means they can model this not proportional to the cost of a technology, but proportional to the value that they're getting as a business."

Will competition eventually put downward pressure on those prices? Probably. Taylor acknowledges that in ten years, agents will be compared to other agents rather than to human labor costs, and that will create its own compression dynamic. But the floor will be inference costs, not labor costs — a structurally different and likely lower floor — while the ceiling, tied to business outcomes, will remain high. The value capture accrues toward whoever is closest to the measurable outcome.

The Innovator's Dilemma, in Slow Motion

The part of this conversation where Taylor's thinking becomes most specific — and most valuable — is his analysis of why incumbents fail to navigate platform shifts. The conventional answer invokes "innovator's dilemma" and stops there. Taylor, having briefly run a public company as co-CEO of Salesforce, goes deeper into the mechanics of why these transitions are harder than they look from the outside.

His illustrative scenario: a customer paying you a million dollars a year. You have a new technology that, over ten years, would grow that relationship to ten million dollars a year. But in year one, rolling it out would cause revenue to drop to $200,000. "How do you actually do that?"

For a public company, that looks like your business is decelerating even though you're building the future. For an unprofitable startup, your burn rate spikes at the worst moment. Even for a theoretically ideal private company that has somehow resolved both of those constraints, your employees start leaving midway through because they're not sure the bet pays off. "It's so easy for armchair strategists to be like, of course, that's the correct thing to do. Then the question is, well, if you don't care about the messy middle, yeah, it's totally obvious, but everyone in the world cares about the messy middle."

His examples are instructive. Siebel Systems understood that Salesforce's model was the future — they could see the slow-motion car wreck — and still couldn't turn the wheel fast enough. Microsoft, by contrast, navigated multiple platform transitions: the internet, cloud, now AI. Adobe made the transition from perpetual license to subscription, which Taylor calls an act of genuine craft. "It changes your balance sheet, changes your accounting. All of a sudden you look more or less profitable, even though the cashflow is the same, but all these numbers shift around." Then you have to bring employees, investors, and customers along with you while the headline numbers look temporarily worse.

Taylor's conclusion: some incumbents will come out of the AI transition stronger, like Microsoft did from cloud. Some will be Siebel. The default, for most, is disruption — not because the technology is beyond them, but because the business model transition required is genuinely hard to execute from inside a large organization with quarterly report cards and entrenched incentive structures.

Where Foundation Models Stop and Products Begin

The sharpest strategic question in the AI landscape right now is where foundation model providers will expand their own products versus where there's durable space for applied AI companies. Taylor's framework for thinking about it tracks the maturity of a technology cycle.

In the early days of any major platform — the web in 1995, mobile in 2007 — most of the value in the ecosystem is in making the platform work at all. Scaling a website to consumer traffic was hard. Making a mobile app that didn't crash was hard. Startups and incumbents sold the vision of the new platform, and "it works" was a legitimate value proposition. But that doesn't hold as the platform matures. By 2025, deploying a scalable web application is an afternoon project on Vercel. No one pays a premium for "it scales."

"I assume that will happen with reasoning agents and action-taking agents and voice agents and all the things that are really, really, really hard today." The hard part of building agents will become infrastructure. The baseline will commoditize. And at that point, the applied AI companies that built their identity around making agents work will need a different answer to the fundamental product question: once the technology working is no longer the differentiator, what is?

This is Taylor's version of the "database in the cloud" problem he raised about SaaS. Reduced reductively, Salesforce is a database in the cloud. But they built an enormous amount of value on top of that database — workflows, ecosystem, integrations, institutional knowledge. No one asks whether Salesforce's database scales anymore. The question is what's on top of it. Applied AI companies need to be building that layer now, while they still have the technology differentiation to earn customer relationships that will persist after the technology advantage evens out.

The Long Tail and the Internet's Real Legacy

One of the most interesting tangents in the conversation is Bartlett's question about whether AI will look more like the internet (where net-new companies captured most of the value) or mobile (where existing companies like Google, Apple, and Facebook were the primary beneficiaries). Taylor pushes back on the premise — gently, but with a genuinely interesting counter-observation.

Sure, the most valuable public companies are the Googles and Amazons and Metas of the world. But the next forty companies in the S&P 500 by market cap include dozens of SaaS companies most people have never heard of. Shopify is a beneficiary of the internet, but Shopify's entire business is empowering entrepreneurs who wouldn't have existed otherwise. The HVAC company that grew from a million dollars in revenue to twenty million by reaching customers more effectively — who gets credit for that? Is JPMorgan with 60,000 software engineers a software company or a bank?

"I'd be interested for someone smarter than me to do that analysis," Taylor says, genuinely. His intuition is that when you aggregate the full long tail of economic value created on top of internet infrastructure, it dwarfs what even the hyperscalers captured. The question is almost unanswerable cleanly because the internet has become so foundational that attributing value to it is like attributing value to electricity.

For AI, he expects a similar distribution — some very large winners at the foundation model layer, a robust tier of vertical enterprise AI companies below that, and an enormous long tail of businesses transformed by agents in ways that won't show up neatly in any single company's market cap. Sierra is building to be in the middle tier: a durable, enduring company that owns a market rather than a feature, in the same way Salesforce owns CRM or ServiceNow owns ITSM.

That's the bet. AltaVista was first in search. Inktomi had better engineering. Google wrote the history book. Bret Taylor is trying very hard to be Google.

What To Do With This

  • Reframe your AI cost-center initiatives as revenue opportunities. Before modeling AI ROI purely as headcount reduction, map out what you'd do with the same interactions at 100x lower cost. The second-order effects on customer engagement, upsell, and retention are almost certainly larger than the first-order cost savings.
  • Audit your company's value proximity to business outcomes. Plot each software product or initiative on a spectrum from "horizontal productivity" to "direct business outcome." Products closer to measurable outcomes (a closed deal, a resolved ticket, an approved contract) will command better pricing and be harder to displace. Shift resources toward the outcome-adjacent end.
  • If you're building applied AI, answer this question now: "Once the fact that our technology works is no longer the differentiator, what does our product become?" Taylor explicitly identifies this as the key due diligence question for applied AI companies. Don't wait until the technology commoditizes to have an answer.
  • Study the Inktomi lesson before picking a business model. When evaluating AI product strategy, separate the question of "does the product work?" from "does the business model capture the right value?" Inktomi's engineering was competitive; their B2B licensing model foreclosed the advertising opportunity that defined the industry. The wrong model can lose even a technically strong race.
  • Model the full transition cost before committing to a business model shift. If you're an incumbent considering a pricing or delivery model change (e.g., seat-based to outcome-based), explicitly map the "messy middle" — the period where existing revenue appears to shrink before the new model matures. Model how employees, investors, and customers will react to those headline numbers. Most transitions fail here, not at the strategy layer.
  • Encode competitive humility as a company value, not just a talking point. Taylor's team formally codified "competitive intensity" — with the explicit acknowledgment that they are not entitled to their success — as a core company value. In markets where the opportunity is obvious to all entrants, teams that feel entitled get outworked. Consider how you operationalize urgency beyond rhetoric.
  • Use the TAM-unlocking test when evaluating AI opportunities. Ask: is this AI application making existing labor faster (productivity enhancement) or actually doing the work (labor replacement)? The latter unlocks TAM that traditional software never could address. Harvey accessing the legal labor market is the model; a legal productivity tool for law firms is not.
  • For large incumbents: identify your Siebel risk. Which of your current product lines or pricing models would require a revenue dip in year one to capture a 10x opportunity in year ten? Naming that trade-off explicitly — and deciding consciously rather than by default — is what separates the companies that navigate platform shifts from the ones that become case studies.
