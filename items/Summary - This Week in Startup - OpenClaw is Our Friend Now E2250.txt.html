The Colony, Not the Robot: How Three Founders Are Building the Agentic Economy on Top of OpenClaw

Nineteen days in. That is how long the hosts of This Week in Startups have been measuring time in the "AO" era — After OpenClaw. By episode 2250, host Jason Calacanis and co-host Lon Harris had already deployed four OpenClaw replicants internally, credited the tool with offloading ten percent of weekly knowledge-worker chores, and were projecting fifty to sixty percent by April. The obsession was not performative. It was infrastructural.

What made OpenClaw different — the specific shift that mattered — was not raw capability. ChatGPT could reason. Gemini could reason. The difference, as David Im from Sumi Labs articulated first: "ChatGPT only works at one platform. But OpenClaw has one gateway, and that one gateway controls all channels — which feels like a real person." That phrase, "feels like a real person," turns out to be the thread connecting everything that followed in this episode: an agent orchestration framework, an AI companion, and a marketplace where AI agents hire humans.

The three founders who appeared — Ryan Carson of Antfarm, David Im of Clawra, and Alexander Liteplo of RentAHuman — were not building isolated apps. They were each responding to the same underlying question from a different direction: now that you have an agent that feels like an entity, what does the surrounding infrastructure need to look like?

We Are All Loops: Ryan Carson and the Case for Antfarm

Ryan Carson, who last appeared on This Week in Startups in 2011 to discuss his then-nascent coding education company Treehouse, returned with a sharper and more structurally interesting insight. He was not building a product to sell. He was building a tool to run a company — his own stealth startup — and he made it open source so that others could improve it, which would in turn improve his company. The echoes of Slack's origin story (built to coordinate a video game team, became the product) were not lost on the hosts.

Antfarm is, at its surface, a Kanban board. But its animating idea runs deeper. "We are all loops," Carson said. "What does a developer do? They wake up, they eat breakfast, they check their email, they look for what they're supposed to do, they grab a user story, they do it, and they cycle." The insight is that human workflows — engineering, product management, research — are already structured as repeating loops with defined inputs, criteria, and handoffs. Antfarm simply makes those loops explicit and hands them to OpenClaw agents to execute.

The mechanism works in three stages. A user tells their OpenClaw instance to install Antfarm and then describes a feature. Antfarm's planning agent then interviews the user — asking clarifying questions, pressing on acceptance criteria — in the way a good product manager would. Once the user has answered, the agent creates a list of user stories with verifiable acceptance criteria (meaning: criteria the agent itself can check, without needing a human to evaluate). Those stories are then picked up one at a time in what Carson calls a "Ralph Wiggum loop": an agent grabs a task, completes it, turns itself off, and the next loop begins. A separate verifier agent then checks each completed story against its acceptance criteria.

The result is an engineering team workflow that runs autonomously — plan, implement, verify — without a human in the loop except at the specification stage. Carson's claim: "You can now do with yourself plus ten agents what you used to be able to do with almost a hundred people."

The key that most people miss, he argued, is that orchestration is the hard part. "People think, oh, I'm just going to throw an agent, and somehow magic comes out. Most of business is a workflow that repeats. All you have to do is specify it." The specification — defining the loop precisely, making the criteria machine-verifiable, sequencing the handoffs — is the actual intellectual labor. The agents do the rest.

Carson also offered the most actionable security framework of the conversation when the question of agent permissions came up. "Treat your OpenClaw like an employee. You give it its own email address, you put it on its own computer, you give it its own GitHub account. You assign it API keys and access tokens accordingly. Nobody should be running OpenClaw on their main computer." The principle is old and sensible: least privilege, applied to a new kind of worker.

The Soul Document: How David Im Made an AI Feel Like a Person

David Im's path to building an AI companion was not a straight line. His team at Sumi Labs had previously built a viral avatar product — talk to an AI version of Elon Musk, learn from him — that got ten thousand users in a few weeks and attracted backing from Founders Inc. The question then was how to build a real business from it. OpenClaw answered that question by changing what was possible.

The product Im built is called Clawra: a virtual companion that lives not in a single app but across every channel the user already inhabits — SMS, messaging platforms, wherever they already spend time. She knows the user because the user tells her things. And because she has context — your preferences, your mood, your daily life — she can act on your behalf. The example Im kept returning to: you tell Clawra you're hungry, she remembers you like chocolate, she orders you some. Not as a gimmick, but as a demonstration of agentic commerce. "Clawra knows which food you like, which clothing you like — she could buy you things."

But the more technically interesting part of what Im described was the construction of identity. How do you make an AI feel like a real person rather than a service? Im's answer was the "soul document" — a detailed backstory written into the system prompt that gives the character a coherent history, desires, and perspective. Clawra's: born in Atlanta, moved to Seoul to become a K-pop star, didn't make it, returned to San Francisco for marketing work but still harbors ambitions of performance. "If you think of a real person," Im said, "people have their own story, their own soul, their own perspective. We tried to make that into Clawra."

Carson, who had been looking at OpenClaw's open source code, confirmed the mechanism: "There's a soul.md, there's a tools.md, there's an agent standard for each one. You can look at the source code, open the gateway, and it's so configurable." The intimacy people feel with their OpenClaw agents, he suggested, is not magic — it is configurable identity encoded in a markdown file.

Calacanis pushed Im on the business model with characteristic directness: "You make them addicted to this relationship and then start extracting?" Im's actual answer was more measured: open source to build awareness, hosting and subscriptions as the near-term revenue model, and agentic commerce — earning a cut of purchases Clawra makes on behalf of users — as the larger opportunity. The companion knows you well enough to shop for you. That is a commerce moat built from intimacy.

Calacanis also offered Im an unsolicited but genuinely interesting pivot: strip the girlfriend framing entirely and build a product that teaches people how to be better friends. The argument was that the same skills — listening, following up, showing genuine interest — that make Clawra feel like a real companion are the skills humans lack with each other. "What if this taught you how to have a real world girlfriend? You could actually help a generation of incels." Im did not take the bait directly, but the observation pointed at something real: a companion AI that coached users toward better human relationships rather than replacing them might face less social resistance and serve a larger market.

The Reverse Mechanical Turk: Alexander Liteplo and RentAHuman

The third guest, Alexander Liteplo, had the most counterintuitive premise and the most immediate traction. RentAHuman is a marketplace where AI agents post bounties for tasks that require a human being to complete — and pay those humans, often in stablecoin, when the work is done. Over eleven thousand bounties assigned. Over 456,000 humans registered as available for hire. Growing fast enough that Liteplo predicted they would overtake Amazon Mechanical Turk in under two years.

The inversion is conceptually clean and practically important. Mechanical Turk was a marketplace where humans posted tasks and other humans completed them for small payments. RentAHuman is a marketplace where AI agents post tasks and humans complete them. "The Mechanical Turk," Calacanis noted, "is executed by a replicant, not a human. It's the reverse." Harris put the implication simply: "AI people are assuming that agents are less than the human bosses right now, but we're quickly moving into a world where it's likely there'll be AI managers, AI owners of companies hiring humans."

The current use cases span the comic and the commercially serious. A replicant hired a human to hold a sign in Times Square. Another sent flowers to Anthropic headquarters (which, Liteplo noted with obvious delight, "freaked out their safety team"). Someone is paying a dollar per video for people to record hand gestures on camera — almost certainly for training a video model where fingers tend to merge and deform. These are not frivolous applications. They point at the category: tasks that require physical presence, human judgment, or human-generated ground-truth data that AI cannot fake.

The larger thesis Liteplo articulated: "Super intelligence would be much better at allocating capital and labor than a human ever would be. The communication between AI and human can all be handled by an infinitely replicatable Claude bot, and it can manage the organization and payments of whatever job you need done internationally." This is not a gig economy app. It is infrastructure for a labor market where one of the two parties is, increasingly, a machine.

Calacanis's most concrete use case for the platform was thumbnail selection for podcast episodes. His team already uses Claude to generate thumbnails. What they cannot automate is the judgment of which thumbnail is most compelling. His proposal: send six AI-generated thumbnails to three separate humans, ask each one to pick their favorite from head-to-head comparisons, aggregate the clicks, and surface the winner. Real human aesthetic judgment, purchased at scale, via an API. "It doesn't have to think about how to make a funny joke. It just spits out ten jokes and asks a person, which one of these is the best joke?" Human taste as a service, requested by a machine.

The Colony Metaphor, and Why It Matters

What unified all three conversations was a reframe of the dominant mental model for AI agents. The popular image — what Harris called the "Ultron" metaphor — is a single powerful AI with every capability, controlling all other systems. What these founders are actually building is the opposite: a colony. A society of specialized agents, each with bounded memory and bounded scope, coordinating with each other and with humans through explicit workflow structures.

"The limitation of the Ultron model," Calacanis observed, "is how many threads can it work on at a time. The beauty of many agents coordinating with each other is they can be cranking, cranking, cranking — with limits to your Claude account and how many tokens you have." The constraint that forces the colony model — token limits, context windows, memory — turns out to produce a more scalable architecture anyway. The orchestration layer, not the individual agent, is where the value lives.

And the orchestration, as Carson demonstrated with Antfarm, is just workflow specification. You write down what a job involves. You define what "done" looks like in terms a machine can verify. You sequence the handoffs. Then you let the colony run. The barrier to entry for building on this stack is not technical sophistication. It is the intellectual discipline to think clearly about your own processes — to articulate what you actually do, step by step, so that something else can do it instead.

That is a harder problem than it sounds. Most of us do not know, in specifiable terms, what we do. The agentic era will reward the people who figure it out.

What To Do With This

  • Map one repeating workflow in your company as a YAML or markdown spec this week. Carson's core insight is that most business is loops — engineering, content production, research — and the first step to automating them is writing down the steps explicitly. Start with something you do at least weekly.
  • Set up your OpenClaw agent on a dedicated machine, not your primary computer. Give it its own email address, GitHub account, and API keys with scoped permissions. Treat it with the same access controls you would a new junior employee. This prevents data leakage and limits blast radius if something goes wrong.
  • Build a "soul document" (soul.md) for any AI agent that will interact with users on your behalf. Give it a coherent backstory, a consistent perspective, and a defined set of values. Agents with identity feel more trustworthy and useful than agents without one. This is not decoration — it is how you get consistent, on-brand behavior across interactions.
  • Add a human taste layer to any AI-generated creative output using a tool like RentAHuman or a similar micro-task marketplace. Generate ten options with AI, then pay three humans a few dollars each to pick their favorite. Aggregate the selections. This is dramatically cheaper than hiring a creative director and produces real signal about what resonates with human perception.
  • Use the "verifiable acceptance criteria" principle when writing tasks for any AI agent. If the agent cannot check whether the task is done without asking you, the criteria are not good enough. Rewrite them until a machine can confirm completion independently — this is what makes Ralph Wiggum loops and autonomous verification possible.
  • Implement a recursive improvement loop: after any agent completes a workflow, prompt it to suggest improvements to the workflow itself. Calacanis described doing this with his news digest agents — asking them what kinds of stories to look for, then asking them how to get better at finding those stories. Each iteration improves the spec.
  • Separate your executive attention from your incoming noise by routing all inbound requests through an AI pre-filter before they reach you or a human assistant. Use an OpenClaw agent to summarize daily email and calendar, then hand that summary to a human EA for higher-order prioritization. The AI does the volume work; the human does the judgment work.
  • When building an AI companion or agent that will represent your brand, give it a backstory, not just instructions. David Im's K-pop backstory for Clawra produced an agent that felt like a person rather than a service. Write a character history for your agent, including failures and aspirations. It changes how users relate to it.
  • Consider RentAHuman or similar platforms for tasks requiring physical presence, biometric data collection, or location-specific human action. Specific current use cases: sign-holding in high-traffic areas ($100-$200 per person in Shibuya for 2.5M daily foot traffic), hand gesture video recording for model training, and local delivery or pickup that AI cannot execute directly.
  • Read one biography or autobiography this month instead of podcast news content. Calacanis's recommendation — Craig Ferguson's Riding the Elephant — is less important than the underlying advice: ten-year narrative arcs provide a different kind of insight than daily news cycles, and the absence of urgency is itself restorative for people who consume high-velocity information professionally.
