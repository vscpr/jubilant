The iPhone App Gold Rush Is Back — and This Time Anyone Can Play

When Pat Walls, founder of Starter Story, sits down to talk with Sam Parr and Shaan Puri, he opens with something that sounds absurd on its face: iPhone apps are one of the hottest business opportunities right now. Not in 2010, when the App Store was new and everyone was scrambling to publish anything with a touchscreen. Right now. Of the twelve founders Pat interviews every week, roughly half of them are making serious money from iOS apps. This isn't something he read on Twitter or generated with ChatGPT. It's what he sees every single week, in the trenches, in the numbers.

The example he reaches for first is deliberately ridiculous: an app called PushScroll that blocks your social media until you do pushups. You hold up your phone. AI watches you do the reps. Then you get to scroll. It's doing $30,000 a month — and that's the disclosed number. A companion app makes you pray before you can open TikTok. "Just like God intended," Shaan notes. Another tracked your nicotine puffs and gamified quitting — it sold for a significant sum before anyone could make the Philip Morris joke.

The question is obvious: isn't this the 2010 opportunity? Isn't this exactly what people tried when the App Store launched? And the answer is yes — but something structural has changed that reopened the window.

The Lollapalooza Effect: Two Tailwinds Arriving at Once

Shaan reaches for Charlie Munger's concept of the Lollapalooza effect: when multiple independent forces line up and push in the same direction simultaneously, you get an outcome that's dramatically larger than any single force would produce. That's what's happening with iOS apps right now, and there are at least two major factors converging.

First, building got cheap. To build a competent iPhone app in 2015, you needed a team of four: designer, product manager, backend engineer, frontend engineer. That team would run you $400,000 to $800,000 a year in salaries alone. A niche app helping people quit vaping would never pencil out. Today, with AI coding tools, a single founder — even a non-technical one — can build a functional, App Store-ready product. It's not perfect, Pat says, but it's 95% of the way there. Apps that simply couldn't have existed before because the economics didn't work are now trivially buildable.

Second, discovery got a new channel. The App Store search algorithm has always been brutal. But TikTok changed how apps get found. A single viral video demonstrating a weird, visually compelling app concept can drive hundreds of thousands of downloads overnight. The PushScroll founders understood this intuitively — in fact, they understood it so well that they reversed the entire product development process.

Fake the App, Then Build It

The PushScroll story is a masterclass in modern product validation. The founders didn't build an app and then try to market it. They created a TikTok video pretending the app existed — acting out what it would look like to be blocked from Instagram until you dropped and did pushups. That video was one of several they made about different app concepts. It was the one that went viral, pulling hundreds of thousands of views. Only then did they scramble to actually build it.

This is, as Shaan puts it, the opposite of Field of Dreams. Instead of "if you build it, they will come," it's: prove they'll come, then build it. The asymmetry is striking. Building ten apps and having none work might take you a year. Making ten videos and seeing which one goes viral takes ten days. The validation cost is almost zero. The signal is real.

For non-technical founders, this model is particularly powerful. You don't need a shipped product to test whether anyone wants your concept. You need a TikTok and a phone. The founder who did the marketing for PushScroll wasn't technical. His co-founder handled the engineering. His job was to be good at pushups — and at creating content people wanted to share.

Apps Are the New Info Products

There's a frame Pat offers that Sam says didn't fully land until he heard it out loud: apps are the new info products. In the early internet era, the dream for aspiring entrepreneurs — the lifestyle business vision — was the info product. Course on how to start a dropshipping store. E-book about copywriting. Membership community around whatever niche you occupied. Low overhead, no inventory, sell it on the beach. The problem was the people selling info products got a reputation for being sleazy.

Apps occupy the same structural role now, but with more legitimacy. The best categories: health, wealth, relationships, productivity, self-improvement. Fitness and gym tracking. Crypto and trading. Habit building. Behavior change. These are enormous, evergreen human problems where people will pay a recurring monthly subscription for something that works — or even just something that's interesting enough to download.

Cal AI is the canonical example. Zach, the founder, came on My First Million during his lunch break from high school. The app used AI to track calories from photos. At the time of the interview, it was doing $30 million a year. By the time this episode was recorded, it appears to be on pace for $70 million. Built by a kid who skipped fifth period to do a podcast interview.

The B2B Video Gap

Pat arrives with several business opportunities. After iOS apps, the one that generates the most conversation is B2B video — specifically, the absence of any functioning system inside most companies for producing video that actually works.

The framing is clean: every major business function has playbooks. Design has playbooks. Engineering has playbooks, especially now with AI. Sales has playbooks so thick they could stop bullets. But video? There's no playbook. Companies know they need it. They see their competitors building YouTube channels and going viral on TikTok. They feel the pressure. And they have absolutely no idea how to execute.

Shaan's framing here is worth quoting directly: "Video is now the native tongue of the internet. It's the language that the internet speaks. And so if you can't make good video, it's like moving to America and not being able to speak English. You might be able to get by, but it's not going to be easy for you."

Companies aren't just bad at video. Most don't even have a job function for it. The task gets handed to the youngest employee, or whoever happens to own a camera, or the social media manager who was already doing four other things. The gap between what companies need and what they have is enormous — and the willingness to pay to close that gap is extraordinary.

Pat knows someone running a YouTube strategy agency targeting Fortune 500 companies: Microsoft, Figma, the big names. The service is essentially: here are the titles, here are the thumbnails, here is the strategy for your YouTube channel. The rate: $50,000 to $100,000 per month. And there aren't many people offering it.

Shaan adds the adjacent plays: a guy named Josh who niched down entirely into man-on-the-street interview content for brands, doing street interviews in New York on commission. An agency that flies to companies, podcasts their CEO for an hour, then chops the interview into clips as if it came from a real show. "Nobody even needs to know there's no long-form podcast underneath this," Shaan explains. You cut out the whole "get booked on podcasts" problem entirely. You just deliver the clips. Ten clients at tens of thousands per month and you're over a million in revenue.

Pat's Video Production System: The Treatment

Because the conversation drifts toward the mechanics of actually making video content, Pat pulls up his production system — a Google Doc framework that Starter Story uses for every YouTube video they make. The centerpiece is something borrowed from Hollywood: the treatment.

In Hollywood, when you're trying to sell a script, you don't hand the director 120 pages. You write a treatment — a sales pitch for the script. A document that answers: why should this video exist? What feeling will the viewer have when it ends? Why will they send it to a friend?

Pat's production workflow starts before filming. Title and thumbnail first — that's the packaging, and it determines everything else. Then the treatment: a brief document that captures the emotional and intellectual premise of the video. Not an intro. Not an outline. A persuasion document aimed at the producer, establishing exactly what they're going for before a camera turns on.

The best videos, Pat says, are built around a single big idea. Not a biography of the founder's life. Not a sprawling narrative. One idea. In the example he shows, the idea is a 10-step SaaS playbook that a founder had written up as a Reddit post — 151 comments, validated by the internet, proven to be interesting before a camera ever rolled. That Reddit post became the skeleton of the video. Pat's team builds around it, then goes into the interview knowing exactly what they need to extract to complete the argument.

He's doing two to three of these a week.

The Ego Business vs. the Actual Business

The conversation takes a more personal turn when Sam pulls up a blog post Pat wrote in December 2020 titled "I Am My Own Greatest Obstacle." At the time, Pat was three years into Starter Story, splitting his attention between the blog — which was doing $8,000 a month — and a software plugin he was convinced would be bigger. He was burnt out. Stuck at the same revenue for nearly a year. Couldn't motivate himself to work on anything.

He couldn't afford a cabin for a think week in the style of Bill Gates, so he got in his car and drove a loop around the American West. No phone. No social media. No email. Just driving and thinking. What he figured out over that week: he was putting 20% of his effort into the thing generating 80% of his revenue, and vice versa. Starter Story was the actual business. The plugin was what he thought he was supposed to be building.

He sold the plugin. He went all in on Starter Story. He doubled revenue in a month. And several years later, HubSpot bought it.

Pat names the trap clearly: the ego business versus the actual business. The ego business is what you think you should be building — the thing that looks impressive, the SaaS with recurring revenue, the thing other people point to as the right kind of company. The actual business is the thing that's already working, already generating momentum, already doing something you're genuinely good at. "Follow the money," he says. "What's making money? What are you truly good at making? What are you meant to build?"

Shaan offers a useful corollary about systems: systems don't create growth. Systems manage your headache as you grow. The mistake most aspiring entrepreneurs make is adopting the operating frameworks of people who already have growth — EOS, rocks, scorecards, L10 meetings — when what they actually need is to find the thing that's working and go all in on it. The system helps you scale something that already has momentum. It cannot manufacture momentum from nothing.

Pat uses a modified version of EOS at Starter Story — rocks every six weeks instead of quarterly, because six weeks better matches the pace of information in a startup. He likes the structure. But he agrees with Shaan's framing: most people watching content about systems are at a stage where they don't need systems. They need to find oil.

The Starter Story Acquisition

The conversation takes place on a strange day for Pat. The HubSpot acquisition of Starter Story is days from closing. Not closed yet — not signed, not wired. He's trying to maintain composure, recording a podcast interview about the business he's about to sell without fully being able to acknowledge that it's happening.

When Sam asks how he approached the negotiation, Pat's answer is revealing. Before any acquisition conversations started, he sat down with ChatGPT and worked through what his walk-away number would be — before he was influenced by any offers, before he had any information about what buyers might pay. He got a number from that exercise. He negotiated to that number. And then he felt 100% certain he should have asked for more. Which, he points out, is probably the correct feeling to have — it means you didn't leave so much on the table that the buyer walked away, but you also left some value for them.

The emotional experience of being close but not done, he says, is its own particular form of suffering. The psychology of a deal requires it to be signed, closed, and wired before you can take yourself to the place where it's real. Until then, you're just waiting.

Starter Story started as a side project in Pat's 20s because he couldn't get any business to work and thought maybe if he interviewed founders he'd find a co-founder or an idea. Eight years later, a company that began as a Google Doc sent to founders asking them to describe their revenue decided the content was real enough to acquire. The key differentiator that he credits: requiring founders to share revenue numbers publicly. Nobody wanted to read a founder story without knowing whether the business worked. The revenue number in the headline changed everything.

What To Do With This

  • Validate your app idea on TikTok before writing a line of code. Create a video demonstrating what the app would look like — even if it doesn't exist yet. The PushScroll founders went viral before building anything, then scrambled to ship. Make multiple concepts, see which one resonates, build the winner.
  • Target health, wealth, productivity, and self-improvement for your iOS app. These are the categories Pat is consistently seeing work across the founders he interviews. They map to enduring human problems people will pay monthly subscriptions to address.
  • Pitch your video before you film it — write a treatment. Before any recording happens, write one page capturing: what's the single big idea, what feeling does the viewer leave with, why will they send this to someone else. This document is for your producer, not your audience. Nail this first.
  • Source your video's backbone from existing validated content. Look for Reddit posts with hundreds of comments, successful Twitter threads, conference talks, or seminar presentations. These have already proven to be interesting to an audience. Build your video around that pre-validated structure.
  • Niche a video format down into a productized B2B service. Street interviews, fake podcast clips, YouTube strategy — pick one format and offer it at scale to companies that can't figure out video themselves. Ten clients at $10,000/month is $1.2M/year. The market is undersupplied.
  • Identify your ego business vs. your actual business. Write down what's making money and what you're spending time on. If those two things don't match, you may be in the same position Pat was — 20% effort into 80% of revenue. Consider a think week: no phone, no email, just thinking. Pat doubled revenue in a month after making the decision.
  • Use your walk-away number before any acquisition conversation starts. Before you have any information about what a buyer might pay, establish the minimum you'd accept using a tool like ChatGPT to help you think it through without bias. Then negotiate to that number. If you feel like you should have asked for more, you probably did it right.
  • Don't add systems until you have growth that needs managing. EOS, rocks, scorecards — these are tools for managing a headache you already have. They don't create growth. If your business isn't already working, adding process will slow you down. Find the oil first.
  • If you use EOS, run rocks every six weeks instead of quarterly. Quarterly is too long for a startup. Six weeks means you can incorporate new information faster and adapt your priorities before they're completely stale.
  • Make 30 videos before you judge whether short-form video "works" for you. Pat describes the first 30 as the learning curve — after that, the mechanics become clear and the shame that holds most people back evaporates. The 200-view jail that kills most TikTok accounts is a persistence problem, not a talent problem.
