The CEO Who Cut His Company in Half — and Didn't Ask for a Single Dime

Sebastian Siemiatkowski walked into a board meeting at Klarna and presented something unusual: an ambitious expansion plan — new banking products, international moves, peer-to-peer payments, trading — and a budget that was going down. Not up. The company had gone from 7,000 employees to under 3,000, a 50 percent reduction driven almost entirely by natural attrition and a deliberate policy of not backfilling. And he was proposing to do more with fewer people than ever before.

"I didn't ask for a single dime to do all this," he told Harry Stebbings on 20VC. "The reason for that is because I've seen the acceleration of AI, and I know we can ship all these things on the existing organization."

The board approved it easily. Of course they did. That's what happens when a CEO shows up with a plan to grow revenues while shrinking costs simultaneously. But behind that meeting lies a much larger argument — one that Siemiatkowski has been making for years, even when it was unpopular, and one that is now reshaping how serious operators think about the relationship between headcount, technology, and business value.

The Real Threat to SaaS: It's Not Vibe Coding

The conversation opens on a question that has obsessed investors since DeepSeek rattled markets: if the cost of creating software is going to zero, where does value live? Siemiatkowski's answer is more nuanced than the usual handwaving about moats and defensibility.

The first wave of disruption — AI making it cheaper to write code — is already underway. But Siemiatkowski argues the second wave will be more damaging to incumbents: the collapse of data switching costs. "So far what you're seeing is you have proprietary data stuck in, for example, the CRM vendor. You may replicate and build the same dashboard in your own tool, but all your data is in there, according to their data model, according to their setup."

The lock-in that has protected Salesforce, ServiceNow, and SAP for decades isn't the software itself — it's the data trapped inside it. But agents are going to solve that. One-click migration, AI-assisted data portability, the ability to pull your entire operational history out of one system and into another. When that happens, the question of what enterprise software should trade at becomes very uncomfortable. Siemiatkowski points to Chegg — once a thriving education platform, now trading at 0.2x revenue as ChatGPT ate its lunch — as a preview of what could happen to software companies that mistake their data moat for a product moat.

"From that perspective, you would argue there is still some unfortunate potential to come down even further," he says of SaaS multiples, currently sitting at 5-10x revenues, which he argues should compress toward the 1-2x of utilities if switching costs truly collapse.

His contrarian bet for investors: data centers are dramatically underinvested. "When you look at inference needing to run 24 hours a day for most of the knowledge worker population and it running for like 1% of knowledge worker population today, I'm like, why the fuck isn't more money going into data centers?"

Why Klarna Killed Its SaaS Stack

Two years ago, Klarna started systematically closing down its SaaS subscriptions. The reason wasn't cost — it was context. To make AI work well, you need to give it coherent, unified information. When your data is fragmented across Salesforce, Jira, Slack, and a dozen other tools, each with its own data model and schema, you can't give an AI agent the context it needs to do a job well.

"We need to provide our AI the best context," Siemiatkowski explains. "If your data is separated in these silos — a little bit in this SaaS, a little bit in that SaaS — it's just harder to provide the appropriate context." The solution Klarna landed on: re-imagine the entire tech stack as an AI-native operating system, where deterministic and probabilistic code are integrated into a single architecture, rather than bolted together.

He illustrates the principle with an analogy from Wikipedia. Wikipedia is the most successful knowledge graph ever built, Siemiatkowski argues, not because of what it does but because of a single design decision: there is no "new" button. If you want to create an article, you must first search. Only if nothing exists can you create. Companies work exactly backwards — someone has an idea, they immediately start building, and nobody checks whether the capability already exists somewhere in the codebase. The result is massive duplication, fragmented context, and systems that fight each other.

AI will eventually force companies to clean this up, not because it becomes smarter but because it becomes economically irrational to duplicate. "It's not going to happen because AI gets smarter. It's going to happen because it makes sense economically to not do what you've already done again."

AI as Compression Technology

One of the most original ideas in the conversation is Siemiatkowski's framing of AI as fundamentally a compression technology. He credits a question he heard at a conference in Yellowstone — how can you fit a frontier model on a USB stick? — as the moment the insight crystallized.

His explanation: when a model is trained, it doesn't store information the way a database does. A database creates a new row every time you record the same fact. A model, trained on the same information repeated many times, doesn't duplicate it — it compresses. Romeo and Juliet exists in a hundred variations across human literature, but the model doesn't store a hundred stories. It stores one love story and encodes the variations as parameters.

"Despite what we humans would like to say, the amount of true novel information and knowledge in human society is quite limited. What we see is repetitions and variations on the same themes over and over again."

The practical implication for enterprise: the same compression effect will apply to corporate data. All those duplicate Salesforce records, redundant Slack conversations, overlapping Google Docs — AI will consolidate them into a single source of truth. The compute required to maintain and query this compressed knowledge base will be dramatically lower than what enterprises currently spend. This is why Siemiatkowski is genuinely uncertain whether the AI infrastructure buildout will be as large as bulls predict. The generation use case (making personalized Star Wars movies with your friends' faces) pulls toward more compute. The enterprise compression use case pulls toward far less.

The Headcount Bet: How to Do More With Half the People

Klarna's trajectory is the most concrete proof of concept in the conversation. Seven thousand employees in 2022. Under three thousand today. Employee compensation per head up nearly 50 percent over the same period. The mechanism was straightforward: natural attrition of roughly 20 percent per year, combined with a policy of not replacing departures. The company didn't fire its way to leanness — it simply stopped hiring into roles that AI was absorbing.

When pressed for a prediction on headcount in 2030, Siemiatkowski goes further than most CEOs would: probably under 2,000, possibly significantly less. The jobs that survive, in his view, are two categories: relationships (the humans in Portland talking to Nike, the account managers in Amsterdam negotiating with Adyen) and the emerging category of premium human customer service — what he calls the "artisan" model.

That second category deserves explanation. When Klarna announced its AI customer service had done the equivalent of 700 agents' worth of work, the headline was interpreted as "Klarna fires 700 people." The more interesting story, which got lost in the noise, was what Klarna did next: they built what Siemiatkowski calls an "Uber model" for customer service, recruiting their most passionate customers — people who love the product and know it deeply — to handle inbound interactions as a gig. NPS from those interactions is, by his account, off the charts.

The framing is counterintuitive but coherent: as AI handles commodity customer interactions, the value of genuine human connection in service goes up. "The future of VIP experience will be the human connection." It's the same dynamic that drove demand for artisan coffee and handmade furniture as manufacturing made cheap versions of everything ubiquitous.

Competing for the Future of Banking

Klarna's longer arc is a story about playing the long game in a market that keeps changing shape. In 2015, when the company was losing the payments wars to Stripe and Adyen, Siemiatkowski sat his team down and made a decision: forget checkout, go after banking. The insight was that the future of retail banking was a digital financial assistant — something that wakes you up in the morning, tells you you're overpaying on your mortgage, and handles the paperwork. A decade before AI made that vision technically achievable, Klarna was positioning itself to realize it.

Today the battlefield has three categories of competitor: tech platforms (Apple, Google, Amazon), neobanks (Revolut, Nubank, Chime), and incumbent banks trying to reinvent themselves. Siemiatkowski's honest competitive assessment: Klarna is twice Revolut's size in customers (110 million vs. 65 million) but lags in daily engagement. The fix is converting BNPL users — currently 28-30 million in the US alone — into core banking relationships through card products and banking services. "We are transitioning these buy now pay later customers into full banking relationship customers at a very, very high pace."

On the question of who wins the US market between Revolut and Nubank, he gives a surprising answer: Nubank's David Vélez, primarily because Revolut has overextended. "Nick is so thinly spread right now" — Dubai, India, everywhere simultaneously — while Nubank is executing a more focused expansion from a profitable Brazilian base. It's the bandwidth argument: banking is not a standard tech product that scales through pure software replication. Regulatory relationships, local trust, and human judgment matter in ways that make spreading too thin genuinely risky.

The Inner Game

The most revealing moments in the conversation come when Stebbings pushes on the personal. Siemiatkowski grew up as an immigrant kid in Sweden, parents divorced when he was eight, father an intellectual who ended up driving a cab and drinking himself to death. The origin story of his ambition was a child's magical thinking: fix money, fix the family.

"Once I got money, it turns out that life isn't like that. Because I gave a lot of money to my father and he used it to drink more."

What's interesting isn't the darkness of the story but what he did with the realization that money wasn't the answer. He didn't give up on building. He found a different source of meaning — the journey itself, the challenge of competing at the highest level, the satisfaction of executing on a vision that most people thought was too ambitious.

When Klarna's valuation collapsed from $46 billion to $6.5 billion and he faced hostile MSNBC interviews calling the company a disaster, his coping mechanism was to put on Queen's "Under Pressure" at full volume and remind himself: this is what he signed up for. "I wanted to play in this level of league. So I also have to cherish and be happy about the fact that I get this fantastic opportunity in life to experience these things."

It's a useful frame for anyone building in a high-stakes environment. The pressure isn't a sign something's gone wrong. It's evidence you're playing the right game.

What To Do With This

  • Audit your SaaS stack for AI context fragmentation. Map which of your tools hold proprietary operational data and model how difficult it would be to give an AI agent coherent access across all of them. This is the real switching cost problem — not money, but context.
  • Build with the Wikipedia constraint in mind. Before starting any new internal tool, system, or data structure, require a search to prove nothing equivalent already exists. This one design principle is why Wikipedia has one article per topic while companies have seventeen overlapping CRM records for the same customer.
  • Forecast headcount as a compression problem, not a growth problem. Model what your ideal team looks like if AI absorbs all commodity tasks, then work backwards. Klarna's method: stop backfilling departures and let attrition do the work, while reinvesting the savings in higher per-head compensation for those who remain.
  • Separate "relationship" roles from "transaction" roles in every function. The jobs that survive AI displacement are those requiring genuine human relationships (enterprise sales, partnership management) and premium human connection (high-touch customer service). Everything transactional is at risk. Audit which category each role in your org actually falls into.
  • Start coding with AI tools before you make investment decisions about AI companies. Siemiatkowski's advice to VCs is blunt: if you haven't built something with Cursor, Claude Code, or Lovable, you don't have the empirical intuition to evaluate AI companies. The gap between watching demos and actually shipping something is enormous.
  • Watch data switching costs as a leading indicator of SaaS disruption. The moment one-click AI-assisted data migration becomes reliable for a category, pricing power for that category collapses. Track which SaaS verticals are seeing migration tooling emerge — that's where the next valuation compression happens first.
  • Price the "artisan premium" in your industry. As AI handles commodity versions of knowledge work, human versions will increasingly command a luxury premium. Identify where in your product or service the "human touch" commands a premium and invest in making that experience genuinely exceptional, not just marginally better.
  • Treat AI as a compression technology when designing your data architecture. The goal isn't to store more information faster — it's to converge on a single source of truth and eliminate redundancy. Design data systems around the principle of "search before you create," and let AI help you merge duplicates and enforce canonical representations.
  • Share AI productivity gains explicitly with employees. Klarna raised per-head compensation nearly 50% while cutting headcount in half. The explicit contract — "AI is going to let us do more with less, and you'll share in that profit" — creates safety and buy-in during a disorienting transition. Model the math and make the commitment visible.
