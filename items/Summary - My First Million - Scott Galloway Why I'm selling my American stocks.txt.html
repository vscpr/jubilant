America Is Expensive: Scott Galloway on Selling U.S. Stocks, the GLP-1 Revolution, and Building a Life Worth Spending

Scott Galloway arrived at Davos after a 27-year absence — not because he had any business there, but because some part of him still needed the validation. He walked the Congress Hall, got a hug from Gavin Newsom, and came home more unsettled than inspired. The American brand, he says, has collapsed from "capitalism, consumption, and Bill Clinton" to "chaos, coercion, and compliance." The US is no longer admired. It's cautiously managed around.

That observation runs like a current through everything Galloway said during his conversation with Sam Parr and Shaan Puri on My First Million. Whether talking about market valuations, intergenerational wealth transfers, the loneliness epidemic among young men, or his political activism, Galloway keeps returning to the same underlying argument: America has been systematically harvested by its oldest and wealthiest citizens, and the bill is coming due in ways that smart investors, parents, and policymakers can no longer ignore.

Why Galloway Is Selling American Stocks

The episode's headline claim is both simple and provocative: Galloway is actively reducing his exposure to U.S. equities. His reasoning is not ideological — it's mechanical.

He starts with cycles. Historically, U.S. markets outperform emerging markets for roughly eight years, then the pattern reverses. America has been outperforming for seventeen years. Mean reversion, he argues, is not a prediction — it's a rounding error waiting to happen.

Then there's valuation. The Buffett Indicator — stock market capitalization as a percentage of GDP — sits at 210 percent. Warren Buffett's preferred comfort zone is around 90 percent. Ninety-seven percent of historical time, the S&P has traded at lower valuations relative to earnings than it does today. "America is an asset," Galloway says. "Every public company outside of America is also an asset. You can own every public company in America for $100 or every public company outside America for $100. Which do you think is better value right now?"

He anticipates the obvious counter: that the S&P 500 isn't really an American index anymore. Apple's revenue is global. So is Microsoft's. So is Google's. He grants this point — it's a thoughtful one — but maintains it doesn't fully explain the valuation gap between U.S. and international markets. "I think Europe is on sale," he says flatly.

Galloway's personal situation compounds his caution. He's been rich three times. He's lost it twice. At this stage of life, asymmetric downside matters more than asymmetric upside. He owns American real estate, runs a business funded by American advertisers, and employs American workers. He's already long America in a dozen non-public ways. Diversifying his portfolio into international equities isn't pessimism — it's portfolio math.

His investment thesis extends beyond public markets. While his venture portfolio returns near zero on a decade-long arc, his most profitable investment has been retail shopping centers — a sector almost everyone in his San Francisco orbit declared dead. He's compounding at 44 percent annually. Burlington Coat Factory just extended its ten-year lease. His investor updates aren't about large language models. They're about Home Goods moving into a vacancy left by Bed Bath & Beyond.

The principle generalizes: "Your return on invested capital is inversely correlated to how sexy and cool an industry sounds." He'll join the members' club for musicians and artists in downtown Manhattan. He won't write a check for it. His friend's SaaS platform for healthcare maintenance scheduling? "Working there sounds like I want to put a gun in my mouth. Take my money."

GLP-1 Drugs: The Most Transformative Technology of the Last 30 Years

Galloway's most contrarian and underrated claim may be this one: GLP-1 drugs are more transformative than artificial intelligence. He's been making this argument for several years, including on an earlier appearance on this podcast where he described injecting semaglutide before it was a trend, calling it "a magical drug" and predicting it would cure alcoholism. He's been sober for twelve years; on GLP-1s, he walked past a brewery and felt nothing.

Now, he'd make it national policy. If he were dictator for a day — he upgrades himself mid-conversation from "president" when someone corrects the framing — his third executive order, after mandatory national service and a $25 minimum wage, would be to issue the largest pharmaceutical procurement in history. He'd go to Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly, demand a billion doses, and distribute them free to every rural household in America. His calculation: cut healthcare costs from $13,000 per capita to $6,500, and the federal deficit is on a credible path toward resolution.

He's not naive about the risks. He notes chemotherapy causes leukemia in long-term survivors. The AIDS cocktail gave patients cholesterol problems. "There's no free lunch." But the accumulating evidence on GLP-1s keeps going in one direction: longer life, reduced cognitive decline, suppressed addiction, lower cardiovascular risk. Every new study, he says, finds the drugs are better than previously understood. He compares their civilizational significance to GPS.

His prediction scorecard from a prior appearance shows partial vindication. He called Apple overvalued — the stock declined. He called GLP-1s bigger than GPT-4 — market cap growth supports it. He predicted mRNA stocks would fall — correct. His call that fast food stocks would suffer as GLP-1 adoption spread has not materialized at McDonald's, though the thesis may simply be early.

The Intergenerational Theft Nobody Wants to Name

Galloway is unsparing on generational economics, and he frames it as extraction rather than policy disagreement. The average 70-year-old is 72 percent wealthier than their counterpart 40 years ago. The average 25-year-old is 42 percent less wealthy. Washington, he says, is "a cross between the Golden Girls and the Land of the Dead, and they keep voting themselves more money."

The fiscal architecture tells the story. A $40 billion child tax credit that would benefit young families was stripped from the infrastructure bill. A $120 billion cost-of-living adjustment to Social Security sailed through without debate. Capital gains taxes and the mortgage interest deduction — both of which primarily benefit asset-holding older Americans — remain structural features of a system designed to protect accumulated wealth at the expense of wealth formation.

His proposed reforms are deliberately bipartisan in their logic, if not their optics. He would eliminate capital gains preferential treatment and apply a single income tax rate to all income regardless of source. He would institute a tax holiday for anyone under 30 on their first $100,000 of income — borrowing from Portugal's model. And he would build: "Instead of drill, baby, drill, it should be build, baby, build." He views NIMBY laws as a mechanism that transfers housing wealth to existing homeowners at the expense of young renters, and cites Minneapolis and Austin as proof that liberalizing zoning reduces costs without collapsing markets.

The Male Loneliness Epidemic and Who Profits From It

Galloway has written and spoken extensively about what he calls the "lost young male" crisis, and the hosts pressed him on where the business opportunity lies. His answer was honest to the point of discomfort: the profitable play is exploitation, not solution.

The companies positioned to make the most money from lonely, disengaged young men are the ones targeting their worst impulses: sports gambling, gaming, and social media platforms that monetize isolation. "The most profitable companies are going to be the ones tapping into an immature prefrontal cortex of an American male." He names Meta as the clearest example — it profits most from the very epidemic it accelerates.

The constructive counterforce, he argues, is primarily public policy rather than private enterprise. He advocates for subsidies to "third places" — bars, sports leagues, experiential venues, golf concepts like Top Golf and Putt Shack — as infrastructure for social connection. He pairs this with housing reform: more density, fewer NIMBY restrictions, an environment where young people can afford to live in communities rather than in isolation.

The real estate observation connects back to his shopping center investments. Strip malls aren't dead — they're being repurposed. Macy's closes; a trampoline park or Top Golf moves in. Experiential retail is replacing transactional retail, and the landlords who understood that transition early captured extraordinary returns.

Economic Security: The Number, and What Happens After You Hit It

Galloway offers a precise framework for thinking about financial independence. Take your annual burn — the amount you need to live the life you want. Multiply by 25. Assume a 4 percent post-tax return. That number, once reached, is when you can begin eliminating what hedge fund manager Barry Rosenstein calls the "should bucket."

Life, Galloway argues, has three categories: things you have to do, things you want to do, and things you should do. The power of economic security is not the cars or the planes — it's the ability to ruthlessly eliminate the should bucket. He flew to Jackson Hole for a $200,000 speech. Got there and missed his son's school performance. Went to Davos because he thought he should. Sat in the Congress Hall wondering why he came.

"I'm still insecure and think that if I get invited back to Davos, I will finally be enough, that I will fill the hole that can't seem to be filled." The candor is disarming from someone who publishes widely on wealth and success. He's describing a condition — status hunger — that doesn't resolve at any net worth.

He's transparent about his own numbers: $300,000 to $400,000 per month in spending, which implies a target net worth around $125 million for true security at his lifestyle. He describes the ratchet effect: young Scott thought $1 million meant retirement. Then he had kids, moved to New York, developed expensive taste, and the number kept moving. He's not ashamed of this — he tells it as a cautionary data point. The lifestyle you're aiming at, be honest about what it actually costs.

His practical advice to someone earlier in the journey: move somewhere cheaper while you build. In 2010, earning $700,000 in Manhattan with two kids in private school and $14,000 monthly rent, he felt broke. He moved to Florida, cut his burn in half, invested the savings discipline over compounding years. "Kind of the rest is history."

Resist and Unsubscribe: Political Action Through Consumer Spending

The episode's final and most politically charged section covers Galloway's activist campaign, which he frames as the only lever of political resistance with real mechanical force. His thesis: the Trump administration responds to markets, not protests. The fastest way to move markets is to target the companies comprising 40 percent of the S&P 500 by unsubscribing from their consumer products.

The math is striking. If you cancel a ChatGPT subscription, you remove $10,000 in market capitalization from OpenAI — because at 40 times revenues, each subscription dollar removed multiplies through valuation. To achieve the same economic impact at Kroger, five families would need to stop buying all their groceries for an entire year.

His targets are specifically companies whose leadership has chosen proximity to power over institutional independence — Tim Cook arriving at the White House with inscribed hard drives, Jeff Bezos paying $45 million for a documentary. "These are the enablers." He draws an explicit historical parallel to German industrialists in the early 1930s who tolerated the rise of fascism in exchange for the promise of crushed trade unions and higher profits.

He's candid about the campaign's current scale. The site gets 100,000 unique visitors a day. Thousands of screenshots. OpenAI is not changing its plans because of it. But he frames this less as a prediction of success than a rejection of passivity. "I'm sick of heckling from the cheap seats." He'd rather act and fail publicly than abstain safely.

His own audit found he was spending $35,000 annually on Uber Lux. He canceled, started taking the subway and UberX, and calculates he could lease a BMW i7 with the savings. The lesson he pulls from this is larger than the politics: most people who examine their subscriptions to big tech platforms will discover they're spending far more than they realized, on far more services than they need. The campaign's secondary value is financial discipline, regardless of its political impact.

On Communicating, Creating, and Building a Media Enterprise

When the hosts ask Galloway how he generates his quotable density — he drops around ten memorable lines per hour of conversation — his answer is characteristically deflating. "It's mostly IP theft." He collects ideas that move him, repeats them until they're embedded, applies them across contexts until they become fluent. The example he gives: "Everything does not demand your judgment. You are entitled to not have opinions on things." He found it somewhere, internalized it, now uses it regularly to resist the Dunning-Kruger trap of punditry.

The infrastructure behind his output — seven podcast recordings a week, two newsletters, a book every eighteen months, regular media appearances — is a 28-person team. PropG Media runs editorial meetings Monday mornings. Writers draft, analysts find data, illustrators build charts, editors refine. Galloway's role is increasingly creative direction and final editing, not solo production. His own flow state is 11 p.m. to 2 a.m. with his dogs, a fireplace, and sometimes an edible. The next morning he decides whether what he wrote is usable.

He was a mediocre boss by his own assessment. He created economic security for employees but wasn't consistently kind, and he confused the Steve Jobs mythology with actual leadership. Now he calls friends when they get arrested and sends money without being asked. "Don't ask people if they need help. Help them." He's explicit that this is overcompensation — catching up for the affirmation he failed to give earlier — but he considers it the right direction regardless of motive.

What To Do With This

  • Audit your portfolio's geographic concentration. If you've been fully invested in U.S. equities through a decade-and-a-half bull run, run the numbers on international diversification — particularly European equities, which Galloway explicitly calls "on sale." The Buffett Indicator at 210% versus a historical comfort zone of 90% is a hard-to-ignore data point.
  • Apply the "25x burn" rule to define your actual economic security number. Calculate your realistic annual spending (not what you spend now, but what you'd need to live the life you want), multiply by 25, and use that as your independence threshold rather than a vague aspiration. Update it honestly as your lifestyle and obligations change.
  • Audit every subscription and recurring payment to big tech platforms. Go through your credit card statements line by line. Galloway found he had three Anthropic subscriptions and seven streaming platforms. The savings are real, and if you want to participate in the Resist and Unsubscribe campaign, resistandunsubscribe.com provides a structured way to do it.
  • Invest in experiential and boring businesses, not sexy ones. Before writing any check, ask whether the investment sounds exciting. If it does, join as a customer, not an investor. Retail shopping centers, healthcare SaaS, unglamorous recurring revenue businesses — these are where Galloway puts capital. Venture in hot sectors is where it stalls out.
  • Eliminate the "should" bucket from your schedule. List every recurring commitment and categorize it: have to, want to, or should. The Barry Rosenstein framework is actionable immediately — just doing the audit surfaces how much of your time is spent on obligation rather than choice or genuine desire.
  • Front-run the loneliness economy by investing in third places and density housing. If you're looking for macro themes with both social and financial tailwinds, Galloway sees subsidized experiential real estate and housing reform as the structural plays. Developers operating in cities rolling back NIMBY laws (Minneapolis, Austin) are worth watching as models.
  • Reduce your cost base during your wealth-building years, even if it means relocating. Galloway's move from Manhattan to Florida in 2010 cut his burn by 50%, and the discipline that followed compounded significantly. If you're in an expensive city and feeling financially strained despite good income, the honest calculation is whether the city is worth the premium at this stage of your life.
  • When someone you know is in crisis, help without asking. The specific behavior Galloway describes: call, say "send me your bank details," and don't make them have to admit need. This applies to friends going through divorce, job loss, arrest, illness — any situation where asking for help feels shameful. Bypass the ask entirely.
  • If you write, keep writing regardless of quality — the communication skill compounds. Galloway got a C in high school English and never stopped writing. His advice is simple: writing is the foundation of all other communication ability, and the way to improve is volume over time, not waiting until you're good enough to start.
  • Develop a personal code and commit to it explicitly. Not a vague value statement — a set of specific behavioral rules you follow regardless of context. Galloway's include: always pay when you're out, don't ask people if they need help (just help), spend money on experiences over things, give away everything above your number. An imperfect code, consistently applied, beats situational ethics every time.
