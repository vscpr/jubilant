The Purse Computer Problem: What It Actually Takes to Replace Your Laptop With a Phone

Allison Johnson, senior reviewer at The Verge, walked into a Samsung event last summer with a laptop that couldn't find the Wi-Fi. Her phone could. So she opened the Z Fold 7's inner screen, pulled up WordPress, and filed her story. That was the lightbulb. Not a demo, not a press release — a moment of pure necessity that reframed what a phone could be. "Hell," she thought, "this is a computer."

That moment sent her down a months-long experiment that she and Vergecast host David Pierce dissected in detail: what gear she tested, what actually worked, where the whole thing falls apart, and what the software industry would need to do to make it real. The answer is closer than most people think — and further than the hardware would suggest.

The Setup: What "Purse Computer" Actually Means

Johnson's framing is precise and worth stealing: this is not "phone as laptop replacement." It is "phone as purse computer." The distinction matters. She is not trying to eliminate her desk setup. She is trying to solve a specific problem: leaving the house with a MacBook Air changes the nature of the trip. The bag gets heavier, the mindset shifts, the logistics multiply. You become someone "on a particular kind of journey." If instead your phone can cover your minimum viable computing needs — Google Docs, WordPress, Slack, email — then your purse contains a computer you were carrying anyway, and you remain free to run an errand, pick up a kid, leave the laptop in the car without anxiety.

Device selection was not complicated. Johnson went straight to the Samsung Galaxy Z Fold 7. The Pixel Fold is fine, she noted, and now has AirDrop-style sharing, but "the Z Fold 7 feels like a normal phone." That's the key variable: carry penalty. A foldable that feels awkward in the pocket is a phone that's bad at being a phone with an occasional computing bonus you'll use twice. The Z Fold 7, despite being only marginally smaller on paper than its competitors, crosses the threshold into feeling like pure upside.

The eight-inch inner display is, as Johnson puts it, "just big enough to run WordPress." It also does something psychological. When she is typing on a real keyboard, looking at a screen that fills her visual field the way a laptop would, her brain stops registering the device as a phone. The cognitive mode switches. This turns out to matter enormously for actually getting work done.

The Goldilocks Keyboard Journey

The moment Johnson decided to buy a keyboard, Pierce raised the obvious objection: haven't you just recreated the problem you were trying to solve? If you're carrying a keyboard, you're carrying gear. You've undone the whole thing.

This is where the experiment gets interesting, because the answer turns out to be: not quite, if you choose carefully. Johnson tested three keyboards. The ProtoArc trifolds into a full-size backlit keyboard with a number pad — impressive, but too heavy and too much. A Samsers passport-size keyboard was light enough but "squirrely," with a hinge in the middle that made typing awkward. The winner was the Logitech Keys to Go, an iPad keyboard that Pierce happened to independently endorse without knowing she'd landed on it.

The Keys to Go is worth understanding because it should not be good. The keys barely travel. It's a sealed membrane thing. It feels, as Pierce put it, "kind of gross and membraney." And yet within thirty seconds of use, you're typing at full speed, the battery lasts three years off a coin cell, and the whole thing weighs almost nothing. Crucially, it's the kind of object you can leave on the back seat of a car without caring if it stays there. That psychological threshold — the "throw it in the back seat and don't worry about it" threshold — is part of what makes this a purse computer rather than a laptop-adjacent annoyance.

The correct mental model for evaluating this setup is not "laptop minus features." It is "Chromebook that fits in a purse." Johnson's actual computing needs when she leaves the house reduce to: Google Docs, WordPress, Slack, basic image resizing. The Z Fold 7 with the Keys to Go covers that list. She was confident enough to commit to leaving her MacBook at home for a weekend trip — "only five percent unsure," she said, which in the context of being a tech journalist who might have to file a story at any moment, is genuine confidence.

Where Android Fights the Future

The hardware problem, Johnson and Pierce both concluded, is essentially solved. The Z Fold 7 has more processing power than the Chromebook she bought five years ago, a state-of-the-art Wi-Fi and cellular chip, and an eight-inch screen that handles split-screen without complaint. Samsung has been permissive to the point of generosity about multi-window arrangements — you can make each window whatever size you want, whether it looks good or not.

The software is where the dream curdles. Pierce articulated the core frustration: Android was supposed to scale from smartwatch to TV. That was the promise. In practice, essentially no apps have done the work to function properly across different screen sizes. "There is nothing in these apps that works properly on anything other than a 6-point-something inch candy bar screen," Pierce said. The Slack tablet app is just the phone app stretched, with columns of white space. The Google Docs Android app is, in Johnson's words, "garbage." And when you try to use Google Docs in a Chrome tab instead, the browser constantly nags you to open the garbage app.

The browser problem runs deeper. What Johnson actually wants is desktop Chrome — the ability to open a full browser with tabs and work in the web versions of the tools she already uses. A Chromebook's whole pitch is that it is a good browser. The Z Fold 7 could be that. Android actively resists it. Pierce had a pointed theory: "I don't think anyone at Google uses a web browser. Everybody is just like, desktop apps are great." Johnson's corollary was equally sharp: "I am convinced that no one at Google has two Google accounts somehow. Because this has been a problem for 20 frigging years."

Profile switching is the other recurring friction. Johnson's phone defaults to her personal Google account. Her work account is present but Chrome keeps demoting it. Every new tab reverts. This is solvable — it takes a few taps to get situated — but it is the kind of friction that accumulates into a mood.

Pierce's broader read was that Google's long-running Aluminium project, aimed at unifying Android across form factors, is the theoretical answer. If Google can thread that needle, the purse computer problem is solved. But it requires everyone — Google, Samsung, individual app developers — to do the work simultaneously. "Every time I use it," Pierce said, "I am just less and less convinced that everybody who is going to need to be on board to pull this off is going to be anytime soon."

The Flip Side: When a Phone Becomes Two Devices

Pierce was simultaneously running his own foldable experiment in a different direction. While Johnson was trying to push a Z Fold 7 up toward laptop territory, Pierce was carrying the Motorola Razr Ultra and pushing it toward a smartwatch.

His argument for flip phones as a form factor: they fit in a pocket, they fit in a hand, you can reach every corner with one thumb. More importantly, opening and closing a flip phone creates a psychological demarcation that flat phones don't. "I am done with my phone now." Pierce found this reduced his phone use in a way he didn't expect.

The problem he ran into was the same Android scaling problem from a different angle. When he closes the Razr Ultra, the outer display lights up — and it just runs full Android apps compressed into a tiny screen. A smushed version of Spotify. A smushed version of Gmail. What he actually wants is a phone on the inside and a smartwatch on the outside: quick weather checks, a tap-back emoji, music controls. Simple input and output. Not the full Spotify app.

A listener question from Drake in Portland — who asked whether a flip phone's closed state could become an AI interface in the style of old speakerphone belt clips — crystallized Pierce's thinking. The most interesting version of the flip phone is not a tiny phone. It is a device that shape-shifts between modes: a full smartphone when open, a push-to-talk Gemini machine when closed, a collar-clipped AI recorder if you want to get ambitious. "If when it was closed, I could press and it was essentially just a Gemini machine, that's pretty cool."

The through-line connecting both experiments: form factor changes the psychological relationship. Johnson said it directly — when you fold the phone open into tablet mode and put a keyboard in front of it, you convince your brain this is a different kind of device. Pierce experienced the inverse: closing the flip phone should convince your brain you're now in a different mode. The hardware already enables this transformation. The software doesn't know it's happening.

Sports Streaming: Cable Is Sports and Sports Is Cable

The episode's second major segment brought in Jacob Feldman from Sportico to take stock of where live sports streaming stands in early 2026, and the picture he painted was of an industry mid-transition, complicated almost beyond description, with a clear winner emerging from the noise.

The first thing Feldman noted is that the Olympics have finally been figured out. For years, the broadcast experience was infuriating — primetime packages built around family backstories, tape delay on events whose results you already knew, no way to watch the events you actually cared about. Peacock's approach this year was to simply provide everything: 400 simultaneous streams, multi-view options, a dedicated curling channel if that's your thing, a Scott Hanson-style red zone for event-hopping. "Choose your own adventure in the best kind of way," Feldman said.

Drones deserve a separate mention because they represent a genuine step-change in broadcast quality. Ex-Olympic athletes are piloting the drones in some sports, bringing expertise about what angles matter and how to translate athletic experience into camera position. The whirring audio is a known issue that producers believe is solvable before the Games end. "We can talk less about the technology and more about yeah, that's a cool shot," Feldman said — which is the sign that a technology has arrived.

YouTube TV's Quiet Power Play

The most structurally important development Feldman covered was YouTube TV's new package tiers announced the day after the Super Bowl. The sports-focused bundle separates Fox Sports from Fox News and TNT from CNN — combinations that have been mandatory for years. The sports plan runs $55 for new subscribers, $65 for existing ones, saving $17-18 against the full package.

The timing was deliberate. YouTube TV churn peaks in February when football ends. Offering a cheaper sports tier right as subscribers are deciding whether to stay, with March Madness and the World Cup ahead, is a retention strategy dressed as a product launch. For the sports-focused subscriber who joined for Sunday Ticket and football, the message is: you don't have to quit, you just have to downsize.

But Feldman's bigger point was about leverage. YouTube achieved this by renegotiating its deals with the major programmers — Paramount, NBCUniversal, Fox — at a moment when YouTube has more power than its content suppliers. The Disney dispute last year proved the dynamic: YouTube walked away and came back on its own terms. This is the Steve Jobs-AT&T relationship, Pierce noted, where one side has so clearly won that the other has to capitulate on form factor.

Feldman's conclusion was blunt: "YouTube is TV and TV is YouTube for a lot of people." And YouTube is only beginning to flex that position. The YouTube and YouTube TV apps on a television are still two separate apps that don't know each other exist — NFL games on one side, NFL YouTube content on the other, no connection between them. Feldman expects that gap to close, and when it does, YouTube's advantage over every other platform becomes structural rather than just scale-based.

ESPN's Complicated Middle Path

ESPN, meanwhile, is trying to serve two masters and pleasing neither cleanly. It still derives enormous revenue from the cable bundle, so it cannot afford to be the service that kills cable. But it wants to be the streaming destination for all sports. The result is a tiered product called ESPN (also sometimes ESPN Unlimited) that costs $30 a month, is free with cable, and exists alongside ESPN Select and ESPN Plus in a structure that Feldman — who covers this for a living — described as something he has to remind himself of each morning.

The NFL Network acquisition brings ESPN seven more games, but they're giving four back, netting three. Red Zone comes with the deal but only for linear distribution — no digital Red Zone in the ESPN app. The MLB TV integration is in progress but fragmented by local blackouts, T-Mobile deals that run through 2028, and authentication configurations that make a simple pitch to consumers impossible. ESPN is "slowly working its way towards a more simple structure," Feldman said, with the emphasis on slowly.

The Real Race: Who Becomes the Front Door

The frame Feldman offered for the current moment is that the old war is over. Streaming beat cable. That question is resolved. The new war is between streamers. Netflix versus Amazon versus YouTube versus ESPN — not streaming versus broadcast.

Amazon's play is distinct from YouTube's. Where YouTube TV is trying to be the one bundle you need, Amazon is positioning Prime Video as the hub through which you buy everything else. Thursday Night Football gives you a reason to already be in the Prime Video app. From there, Amazon sells you Peacock, Paramount Plus, and eventually ESPN. The thirty percent platform fee structures the economics. Amazon doesn't need to own every sport; it needs to own just enough sports that you default to its interface, and then monetize your path to everyone else's sports.

The endgame Feldman described has one of two shapes: either every sport stratifies to a single provider (Apple has F1 and MLS; someone else has the NFL; someone else has MLB), or the dispersion gets so extreme that one aggregator wins by making navigation possible. The NFL rights renegotiation happening now will determine which direction the sports streaming universe bends. "The first companies you named as the most interesting players for the NFL," Pierce noted, "are all tech companies." The traditional broadcasters — CBS, Fox, NBC, ESPN — are now the also-rans in that conversation.

For sports fans in 2026, none of this has resolved into simplicity. Knowing where to watch all NFL games remains, as Feldman put it, his job specifically — not something any normal person should be expected to track. "Next year it will be simpler," he said, acknowledging they've been saying that for ten years. One day it will be true.

What To Do With This

  • Try the Logitech Keys to Go before any other portable keyboard. It defies expectations despite mediocre key travel — you'll be at full typing speed within thirty seconds, and the coin-cell battery runs three years. It is the difference between a setup that stays in your bag and one you actually use.
  • Open Slack in a Chrome tab on your foldable, not in the app. The Android Slack tablet app is just the phone app stretched. In Chrome, you get proper column layout. The same applies to many productivity apps — always try the web version before assuming the app is the correct tool.
  • Evaluate your "leave-the-house" computing needs separately from your desk needs. Johnson's framing is useful: list the five things you actually need to do when you're out of the house. If that list is Google Docs, email, Slack, and basic image editing, a foldable phone with a compact keyboard probably covers it — and the question becomes carry cost, not capability.
  • Use the Z Fold 7 specifically if you're testing foldable-as-laptop. The size and weight differential from other foldables translates to daily carry feel, which determines whether you actually use it or revert to your laptop out of habit.
  • Set up your work Chrome profile aggressively before leaving the house. On Android, Chrome defaults to personal accounts and reverts on new tabs. Pin your work profile, set it as default before your session, and reduce the number of context switches needed mid-work.
  • If you're on a flip phone, lower your expectations for the outer display — and advocate for better. Current Android software treats the outer screen as a small phone rather than a smartwatch. Until that changes, use it only for notifications and music controls, and don't expect app parity. Report the friction to manufacturers — this is a solvable product design problem.
  • For sports streaming, audit your actual viewing calendar before subscribing or downgrading. The YouTube TV sports tier at $65 makes financial sense if your viewing is football plus one or two spring sports. Run the math against what you currently pay versus what you actually watch — February is the optimal moment to make this adjustment before March Madness.
  • Watch the Winter Olympics on Peacock rather than broadcast if you have it. The 400-stream approach, multi-view, and dedicated single-sport feeds represent the correct way to consume a sprawling multi-event competition. The curated primetime broadcast exists for casual viewers; the app is for people who want to choose their sport.
  • Track the NFL rights renegotiation in 2025-2026 as a leading indicator for where all sports streaming goes. Whether Netflix, Amazon, or YouTube wins significant NFL packages will determine who becomes the default front door for sports content across all properties — not just football.
  • If Android multi-account switching frustrates you, create a dedicated browser session or user profile rather than fighting per-tab account selection. Chrome on Android does support multiple profiles; setting one up for work before a session prevents the constant reversion to personal defaults that breaks the "phone as computer" flow.
