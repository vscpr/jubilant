Burying the Last Comfortable Illusion About China

The first myth fell years ago: that China cannot innovate. Dan Wang, author of Breakneck: China's Quest to Engineer the Future, considers that one buried and is glad for it. The second myth is now on life support: that China can scale but not originate — that the zero-to-one creative leap belongs exclusively to the West, and the Chinese are merely excellent at going from one to a hundred. Wang wants to kill this one too, and he brings receipts.

"Numbers are continuous," he says, with the blunt patience of someone who has made this point seventy-odd times on a book tour. The distinction between innovation and scaling is a Western security blanket, not an empirical observation. Bell Labs invented solar panels in New Jersey in 1957. Bell Labs no longer exists. China owns the entire solar supply chain — polysilicon processing, wafer production, module assembly, and the equipment to make all of it. The zero-to-one inventors lost to the one-to-a-hundred scalers so completely that the original invention is irrelevant. "What does it matter," Wang asks, "if you go from zero to one, if you cannot go from one to a hundred?"

DeepSeek, EVs, factory robotics, semiconductor manufacturing equipment — the pattern keeps repeating. Wang estimates roughly half of the serious people he spoke with on his book tour are still clinging to the zero-to-one distinction. He finds this increasingly hard to defend. "It starts sounding a little bit absurd. They can make all the toys and t-shirts, and they can make the large-capacity batteries for the EVs, but they can't..." The sentence doesn't need finishing.

Engineers vs. Lawyers: A Framework That Went Viral

The conceit that made Wang's book a Financial Times Best Book of 2025 — and that Adam Tooze was already using in London Review of Books keynotes without attribution — is the distinction between the engineering state and the lawyerly society. China is governed by engineers trained to execute. The United States is governed by lawyers trained to argue.

Wang is careful to note the idea wasn't original to him. Bill Clinton quipped about it in 1996 standing in China. But what Wang did was weaponize it — turn it into a framework sharp enough to actually explain how China's government operates and why, and how America's dysfunction looks from the outside. His preparation was almost absurdly perfect: years in Beijing, Hong Kong, and Shanghai analyzing Chinese semiconductor and manufacturing companies for Gavekal Research under Arthur Kroeber; then living through zero-COVID, a policy whose ambition was right there in the name; then moving immediately to a fellowship at Yale Law School, embedded among the people who would staff the Biden administration. Jake Sullivan. Gina Raimondo. Brian Dees. All Yale Law.

The contrast was visceral. Zero-COVID had the mathematical clarity of a missile engineer's optimization function — one of China's foundational zero-COVID architects was indeed a cybernetics expert. The lawyers at Yale operated differently. They were trained to make the best argument for whatever position they needed to occupy.

When host Steve Hsu pushes back — shouldn't scientists and engineers be frustrated with lawyers rather than deferential? — Wang flips the challenge. "Rather than decrying the lawyers for having seized all that power for themselves, I would say, why don't we point the finger at the economists, who had power tenuously, but then gave it up... Why don't we say to the engineers and the scientists, can you not get better at organizing society?" Power, as Nancy Pelosi once noted, is not given. It must be seized.

Wang is not naive about the engineering state's failures. Zero-COVID was a perfect expression of technocratic logic followed to a catastrophic conclusion — 25 million people locked in Shanghai because the math said zero. The One Child Policy was similarly the product of engineering-state thinking. Wang's prescription is pluralism: "What we want is pluralism. What we need are some scientists in the ruling elites, we need economists, lawyers, humanists." The problem with America is not the presence of lawyers. It is the near-total absence of everyone else.

The Industrial Party: An Online Meme That Became State Policy

Sometime in the early 2010s, before the Great Firewall had fully clamped down, a loose movement emerged on the Chinese internet. It had no party affiliation — there are only eight tolerated political parties in China, all subservient to the Communist Party, and this was not one of them. It was more of a meme, a current of thought advocating technocratic governance aimed at restoring Chinese scientific and industrial supremacy.

The Industrial Party, as it came to be known, produced canonical texts. One was called "Study or Wash Dishes." Another, "The Morning Star of Linggao," was online fan fiction imagining present-day people sent back to the Ming Dynasty to industrialize the island of Hainan. But Wang argues the deepest canonical text was Liu Cixin's The Three-Body Problem — humanity banding together under technocratic rule, building enormous navy-controlled spaceships to confront an existential extraterrestrial threat. That is the Industrial Party's political fantasy made literary.

Wang sees this worldview as deeply rooted in China's reading of its own history. For centuries, China understood itself as the preeminent civilization. Then, in a period it calls the Century of Humiliation, technologically superior barbarians — British, Dutch, eventually Japanese — arrived and inflicted extraordinary damage. The lesson absorbed: science and technology are what the barbarians used to beat us, and science and technology are what we must master to reclaim our place. This story, Wang notes, was told before the Communist Party and has been amplified by it into the bones of every Chinese person.

Wang offers a sharp counter-note: "What about the quarter century of self-humiliation that the Communist Party inflicted right afterwards?" The Great Leap Forward. The Cultural Revolution. The One Child Policy. The Strike Hard campaigns. These were not inflicted by barbarians. They emerged organically from within. The Industrial Party narrative conveniently omits this chapter.

The Ferocity No One Talks About

One of Wang's most striking claims, delivered almost as an aside: China embodies greater capitalist competition and greater capitalist excess than America does today. This from a country that still officially espouses Marxism.

American tech, Wang observes, is something like a gentleman's club. Google has search. Amazon has e-commerce. Facebook has social. They don't tread aggressively on each other's territory. Peter Thiel's famous dictum — competition is for losers, monopoly is the goal — is essentially the operating philosophy of Silicon Valley's biggest incumbents, and their political influence is sufficient to ensure the government mostly leaves them alone.

In China, everyone is fighting everyone over everything. Kyle Chan's chart of interlocking competitive relationships among Chinese tech companies looks like a circuit diagram from a nightmare. The result: profit margins driven toward zero, consumers with extraordinary buying power, and a Shanghai Composite index that has traded sideways for twenty years. Bad for Chinese equity investors. Spectacular for Chinese consumers. A wealthy American venture investor living in Beijing told Hsu he simply cannot replicate his quality of life in the United States, even as a billionaire. The food delivery, the services, the sheer purchasing power — none of it maps to what money buys in California.

The irony Wang highlights: China arrests Marxist labor organizers. There are no real unions. The workers have no protection from the relentless competitive pressure the party deliberately fosters. "What sort of socialism is this?"

Preparing Without Eagerness, Waging Without Preparing

Wang has a quote — one of his most cited — that he attributes to Viennese satirist Karl Kraus: "In Berlin, the situation is serious but not hopeless. Whereas in Vienna, the situation is hopeless but it is not serious." He applies this to the US-China cold war. Beijing has been preparing for Cold War without eagerness for waging it. Washington wants to wage a Cold War without preparing for it. America is Vienna.

China grew extraordinarily wealthy under the American-built liberal international order — becoming a major maritime trading power without building a large navy, which Wang notes may be historically unprecedented. Britain, Portugal, the Netherlands, the United States — all built navies to become trading powers. China got the trading power for free, underwritten by American security guarantees. That system is now turning against China. The Chinese are reacting, not initiating.

Meanwhile China has been quietly preparing for the siege: the Mayor's Vegetable Basket Program ensures farmland rings every major city for food self-sufficiency. Energy self-sufficiency is a stated national priority. Technological self-sufficiency — the semiconductor drive — was accelerated precisely by American export controls. Wang has been openly critical of those controls for years, writing in the New York Times and Foreign Affairs that they would ultimately stimulate Chinese self-sufficiency rather than prevent it. He appears to have been right.

The American failure, as Wang diagnoses it, is an unwillingness to pay the high costs required to actually compete. Reshoring manufacturing, rebuilding industrial capacity, funding science — these require sustained generational commitments. "America is not a serious country in a lot of different ways," he says, not cruelly but clinically.

Authoritarianism and Science: A More Complicated Relationship

One of the cherished beliefs of the lawyerly society is that political freedom is a prerequisite for scientific innovation. Free speech enables the productive collision of ideas. Suppression stifles breakthroughs. Wang interrogates this and finds it considerably more complicated.

He cites Harvard historian of science Lauren Graham, who studied the Soviet system and found something counterintuitive: the two most catastrophic events for Soviet scientists were the Stalinist purges of the 1930s and the dissolution of the Soviet Union in the 1990s. Which was worse? The dissolution. When the Soviet Union fell, the money stopped. Labs closed. Scientists emigrated or grew potatoes to survive. During the Stalin purges, by contrast, scientists staggered out of gulags and produced Nobel Prize-winning work, because Stalin funded science lavishly. "When you throw a lot of money at science, you're probably going to get a lot of science."

Hsu corroborates this from direct experience: Soviet mathematicians and physicists who emigrated to the US after 1991 were uniformly nostalgic for the closed system. Secret cities adjacent to weapons programs, where scientists wanted for nothing — food, salaries, orchestras, chess — in exchange for avoiding topics like the Ukrainian wheat harvest. The constraint was narrow. Within it, complete intellectual freedom. Hsu found their seminars ran for hours, fighting to the bottom of every question.

Wang will not say authoritarianism is good for science. What he will say: money is good for science. The Trump administration is cutting scientific funding. University budgets are contracting. China is funding science aggressively. "I think they're going to get the science."

The deeper point is structural. A physicist obsessed with improving a language model's attention mechanism is not thinking about politics. He is thinking about the problem. He can do that in Beijing as readily as in Mountain View — and in Beijing, unlike at Stanford, he can likely get direct access to frontier model training because Chinese universities and companies are more tightly integrated than their American counterparts.

Automation, AI, and the Danger of Magical Thinking

There is a fantasy circulating in American policy circles: that AI and automation will close the gap with China's industrial machine without requiring the hard work of actually rebuilding industrial capacity. Wang is not buying it.

Chinese factories are, on average, ahead on automation. Chinese Tesla workers are more productive than California Tesla workers. China installs as many industrial robots per year as the rest of the world combined. And China has massive advantages in AI training data from its manufacturing operations. The idea that American AI investments — currently approaching 2% of GDP in data center and compute CapEx — will magically solve the robotics gap, the port automation gap, the assembly line gap, strikes Wang as a category error. "We have to be careful not to let automation, like superintelligence, become an excuse for magical thinking rather than doing the hard work of capacity."

On the longer-horizon AI question — recursive self-improvement, existential risk — Wang is neither dismissive nor apocalyptic. He believes the recursive inflection point is real but not proximate, probably five to ten years out. He has participated in war games at the Tate Modern, led the China team against Stanley McChrystal's US team, and watched as both sides, facing the recursive moment, negotiated slowdowns and mutual lab inspections. That outcome gave him some optimism — not because it solves the problem, but because it demonstrates the possibility of the narrative.

He notes that Dario Amodei and Demis Hassabis, at Davos, both said they are in communication and watching for the recursive inflection point together, and both expressed a preference for slower timelines. They would slow down if they could. The problem is collective action: it only takes one lab — one nation, one billionaire with a data center in the UAE — to defect from any slowdown agreement. "It's a little bit like global warming."

Wang ends with a point that loops back to the lawyers-versus-engineers frame. Scientists and engineers need to get better at narrative. The war game at the Tate Modern produced an optimistic ending precisely because narrative matters — because the ape brain responds to enacted story in a way it does not respond to technical arguments. "We are all ruled by ape brains. And so this is why the lawyers are in charge. And the scientists and engineers need to get better at narrative."

What To Do With This

  • Retire the zero-to-one / one-to-a-hundred framework when assessing Chinese competition. It is now empirically indefensible and serves mainly to produce complacency in Western strategy and investment.
  • Track where elite Chinese AI researchers are choosing to work, and ask why. The 90% retention figure in the US is real but may not hold; the Beijing academic-industry integration is a genuine structural advantage that could shift talent flows.
  • Study the Industrial Party's canonical texts — especially The Three-Body Problem as political philosophy, not just science fiction — to understand the motivating worldview of Chinese technocrats and the engineers who build for them.
  • Assess any American AI or automation investment against the specific industrial gaps China leads in (port automation, factory robotics, EV supply chain) rather than assuming general AI progress closes those gaps automatically.
  • Evaluate US semiconductor export controls against their actual effect: Wang's long-standing argument — that they stimulate Chinese self-sufficiency rather than preventing it — is tracking as correct, and strategy should update accordingly.
  • When evaluating scientific productivity claims about authoritarian systems, ask whether the constraint is narrow or totalizing. Soviet scientists worked well inside a narrow constraint; the analogy to China is not perfect but is closer than Western commentators typically acknowledge.
  • Take Chinese consumer market purchasing power seriously as a talent retention tool. Quality of life for high earners in Chinese cities, driven by ferocious service competition, is a real factor in decisions about where to live and work.
  • Do not treat AI as a substitute for industrial policy. Capacity building in manufacturing, logistics, and physical automation requires sustained multi-decade investment regardless of what language models can do. Wang's warning against "magical thinking" is the most actionable policy insight in the conversation.
  • Build or support channels for AI lab coordination across geopolitical lines now, before the recursive inflection point. The collective action problem becomes much harder to solve at speed; the infrastructure for cooperation should be established in advance.
