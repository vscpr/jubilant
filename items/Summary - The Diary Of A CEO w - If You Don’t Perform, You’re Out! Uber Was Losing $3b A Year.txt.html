The Man Who Ran Into the Fire

Most CEOs inherit good companies and keep them going. A much rarer breed takes a burning wreck and builds something genuinely great. Dara Khosrowshahi — the Iranian-born, Brown-educated engineer who turned Uber from a company hemorrhaging $3 billion a year into one generating $9.8 billion in free cash flow — is that rarer breed. But the more interesting story is not the turnaround itself. It is the philosophy that made it possible: a set of convictions about honesty, hard work, transparency, and organizational design that Khosrowshahi has assembled over four decades, forged first by personal upheaval and then sharpened across two transformational CEO stints.

The episode of The Diary of a CEO where Steven Bartlett speaks with Khosrowshahi is ostensibly about Uber — its culture, its pivot to profitability, its looming collision with autonomous vehicles and AI. But at its core, the conversation is about what it actually takes to lead a large organization through chaos, and why most leaders fail not from bad decisions but from bad information.

Forged by Loss

The fingerprints of Khosrowshahi's leadership style are traceable directly to 1978 Tehran. His family ran a large industrial company in Iran — one his father had built with genuine pride. The Islamic Revolution stripped them of everything. Revolutionary guards came to their neighborhood. Bullets went through the living room. His family fled to the United States when Khosrowshahi was nine years old, rebuilding from zero in Tarrytown, New York.

"At my core, I never feel safe," he says. "That feeling of having the rug pulled out of you — of building everything — that's a feeling that never leaves you."

His father — a stoic, humble man who had commanded deep respect from thousands of factory workers — was psychologically destroyed by the loss. He was then trapped in Iran for six years without an exit visa, unable to return to his family in New York. His mother, who had never worked before, became a salesperson to support the family. His father returned diminished, older, and had a heart attack on the plane flying back.

Khosrowshahi watched all of this and drew two conclusions that would define his career. First: never stop building, never stop moving, because the moment you take anything for granted, the rug comes out. Second: the ability to create jobs — to be, as his father was, someone whose arrival at the factory was greeted with genuine respect and affection — matters more than accumulating wealth.

"Making money was important. I don't want to BS and say I didn't care about it," he says. "But as I matured in life, building an enterprise and having that connection with the team — that became really important to me."

The Investment Banker's Lesson: Bet on People, Not Companies

After Brown (bioelectrical engineering), Khosrowshahi spent eight years in investment banking — first in risk arbitrage, then M&A advisory — where he absorbed a lesson from Herbert Allen that he has carried ever since: always bet on people, not companies. "Companies go, they're good companies, bad companies, but great people stay great all the time," Allen told him.

What he looks for in people has remained consistent: success, honor, loyalty, and follow-through. Hard work he considers almost definitionally embedded in success — if someone has succeeded, they worked hard to get there.

The banking years also gave him a framework for spotting transitions. He and Barry Diller — the media mogul who became his most important professional relationship — identified the pattern: retail and phone commerce was migrating online. They went after businesses with electronic transactions requiring no physical fulfillment: travel (Expedia, Hotels.com), ticketing (Ticketmaster), and dating (match.com). They consistently overpaid for acquisitions based on what the market thought, not what the companies turned out to be worth.

"Humans think about success and transitions in a linear way because time is linear," Khosrowshahi explains. "But company success, especially with new technologies — these companies move in an exponential way. Whereas most people project things going like this" — he gestures flatly — "what actually happens is this." The hockey stick. The spread between those two curves, he says, is where fortunes are made.

The Transparency Doctrine

When Khosrowshahi became CEO of Expedia, he was, by his own admission, primarily a financial executive. What shaped him into an operator was being forced to run expedia.com directly — the company's largest business — after going 0-for-2 hiring executives to lead it. He ran both the holding company and the main business simultaneously for five or six years, and it was during that period that he discovered his real passion: operating companies, not just allocating capital.

He also learned something about information flow that has become the philosophical center of how he leads. Three months into his Expedia operating role, his head of HR told him he was scaring people. He was being unusually blunt about the company's real problems — its codebase was old, the technology leadership was coasting, and the company was on a path toward exponential decay, not growth. He chose to keep scaring people.

"Usually the failures I see with CEOs aren't because they made the wrong decisions. It's because they were getting the wrong data that led to the wrong decisions," he says.

His solution is what he calls a transparency doctrine that runs in both directions. A CEO who sanitizes reality for their team cannot expect the team to surface uncomfortable truths upward. The moment employees see their boss bullshitting them, they start bullshitting back. So Khosrowshahi's rule is simple: he tells people what's actually going on, even when it's alarming — because that's the only mechanism that keeps the real information flowing back to him.

He also maintains what he calls "random direct channels" — irregular meetings with engineers four levels below him, young employees who haven't yet learned to filter for authority. Engineers, he notes, tend to have a healthy disrespect for hierarchy: their value is in code, not in managing up. "I find a lot of engineers have a personality which is, I'm going to tell you what it's like." He cultivates those relationships deliberately, treating them as bypasses around the organizational filters his senior staff inevitably apply.

The Uber Turnaround: Honesty as Strategy

When Khosrowshahi arrived at Uber in 2017, the company was a genuine disaster — culturally poisoned, operationally chaotic, losing billions. The offer came partly through Daniel Ek, the founder of Spotify, who said to him over drinks at the Allen & Company conference: "Dara, since when is life about being happy? It's about making impact. Uber is a great company and you can have an impact on that company. You've got to do this."

His father put it more simply: "A company who's a verb tells you to run it, you just say yes."

The culture reset at Uber involved discarding the original values, which had become weaponized. "Toe stepping" — a value meant to encourage candor — had become an excuse to be a jerk. Khosrowshahi replaced the value set through a combination of crowdsourcing and personal conviction. The one value he wrote himself, unchanged to this day, is: Do the right thing, period. No elaboration, no sub-bullets. He was deliberately ambiguous. "Use your judgment," was the message to the whole company. "And it's our expectation that you use your judgment to do the right thing."

The other surviving and new values — "Go Get It," "Great Minds Don't Think Alike," "Embrace the Grind" — were chosen because they describe something genuinely true about Uber, not because they were generic corporate virtues. "Passion, ambition, teamwork — like, what company doesn't believe in teamwork? Give me a break," he says.

The financial turnaround came from the same philosophy applied operationally. He is explicit that Uber is not a brand or a movement — it is, fundamentally, a machine. Every team has specific numerical goals, tracked religiously. The whole company is organized around continuous improvement: the payments team optimizes fraud and success rates, the mobile app team works on conversion, the matching algorithms get refined constantly. "There isn't a piece of the company that is not improving every single day."

One of the most counterintuitive bets that worked: taxis. Uber's founding team viewed taxi companies as enemies — literally fought them in city after city. When a product leader named Sachin proposed building a taxi integration into Uber, the founders told Khosrowshahi it was the most idiotic thing on earth. He was "kind of ignorant," he says, so he let them try. Taxis are now the fastest-growing segment of Uber's business.

Working Hard Is a Learned Skill — And One That Compounds

Khosrowshahi has strong and specific views on work ethic. He thinks people focus too much on what to study, what career to choose, what industry to enter — and not nearly enough on building the capacity for sustained, relentless effort. "The most important skill in life is the skill of working hard," he says. He means it literally: he considers it a skill, not a trait, which can be built deliberately.

He is also honest about its costs. He wakes at 5:30 a.m. checking email. He checks again at 9:30 p.m. But he is religious about having dinner with his family when in town — and sees no contradiction between that flexibility and intensity of effort. The Uber message to prospective employees is explicit: "You come to Uber, you're going to work your ass off. If you're not performing, we're going to let you know. And if you don't fix it, we're going to push you out." He did not make that message this clear at Expedia because Expedia was selling vacations. The cultural signaling had to match the product.

He does acknowledge a genuine tension: he has never personally witnessed someone who was not a hard worker become an exceptionally hard worker. He suspects — as does Brian Chesky and Elon Musk, he notes — that the capacity for extreme effort is heavily shaped by early formative experiences. His own was shaped by watching his father lose everything and never fully recover, and by a nine-year-old's vow to rebuild what was lost.

AI and the Displacement Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Khosrowshahi is more candid about AI disruption than almost any other sitting CEO in public discourse, and he knows it. A CNBC anchor once told him she liked him because he actually answered her questions. He treats that as the only acceptable way to operate.

Uber has been an AI company from day one — routing, pricing, matching, courier batching decisions — all driven by machine learning models stitched together across the physical complexity of 40 million daily trips in Lagos and New York simultaneously. About 90% of Uber's engineers now use AI tools, and roughly 30% are power users showing measurable productivity jumps. Khosrowshahi's current posture: if his average engineer became 25% more efficient, he would hire more engineers because there are still unsolved problems. But five years from now, he may stop adding engineering headcount and instead buy more GPUs and deploy more agents.

On the societal question, he is direct in ways that are unusual: "AI will be able to replace the work that 70 to 80 percent of humans can do over the next 10 years. Ten years is not a lot of time for society to adjust to that kind of impact." Intellectual jobs first — roughly a decade. Physical jobs — drivers, couriers — 15 to 20 years, because the physical world requires more capital and more regulatory work.

Uber has 9.5 million drivers and couriers on its platform. Khosrowshahi does not pretend the autonomous vehicle transition is not coming for them. He says bluntly: "I don't know" what those nine million people do. He points to historical precedent — farming fell from a massive share of labor to under 1% — but acknowledges the critical difference: AI is disrupting both intellectual and physical labor simultaneously, which has never happened before. Every previous technological transition disrupted one category of work and left human advantage intact in the other.

His one concrete prescription: "The retraining machine." He does not see any government or institution genuinely investing in the infrastructure to retrain large populations at scale, and he believes that infrastructure — not universal basic income, which every test has shown produces worse outcomes — is the right answer. People who succeed feel they are creating value. Government-administered income without work does not replicate that feeling.

The personal resonance is not lost on him. His father's devastation after losing his company and his status was not purely financial. It was the loss of meaning — of being someone who mattered to the people around him. "Jobs aren't just about money," Khosrowshahi says quietly, in one of the most honest moments of the conversation.

Career Advice From Someone Who Didn't Plan

Khosrowshahi ended up where he is having planned none of it. His advice to his own four children, and to young people generally, is counterintuitive: stop planning so precisely. "People who have too clear a career plan — they're looking for signals that feed into their career plan. They're not looking around. They're not being curious."

He describes his own trajectory as a sequence of openness to unexpected signals — from banking to Diller to operating Expedia to Uber — none of which was part of a five-year plan. "Before you go out and try to change a world, let the world change you first." The one constant he identifies in every successful person he has ever known: they worked harder than the people around them. Everything else — timing, luck, opportunity — matters, but only if you were open enough to see it and hard-working enough to act on it.

When Bartlett asks what single conversation he would go back and have, Khosrowshahi pauses. He moved from San Francisco back to New York to be near his father at the end of his life, but by then his father was losing his mental faculties. The conversations he wanted — about his father's younger life, his ambitions, his regrets — were no longer possible. He frames his subsequent practice of genuine, honest connection with colleagues, friends, and family as his correction for that loss. "Having those genuine conversations and connections is my way of correcting for the conversation I never had with my dad."

What To Do With This

  • Tell your team the uncomfortable truth before you expect them to tell you theirs. Khosrowshahi's transparency doctrine runs both ways: leaders who sanitize reality for their teams cannot expect honest information to flow back up. If you want real data, model radical candor first.
  • Build deliberate bypass channels to ground-level employees. Schedule irregular, direct meetings with people four or more levels below you — particularly engineers or individual contributors unlikely to manage upward. They will tell you things your senior staff will not.
  • Frame your culture contract honestly before people join. Khosrowshahi is explicit with Uber candidates about the work intensity expected. Mismatches at hire are expensive; honest job previews are cheap. State your actual culture, not an aspirational version of it.
  • When you lose, call it clearly and move on fast. His model from Barry Diller: "They won, we lost, next." Recognize the loss, extract the lesson, stop dwelling. Obsessive post-mortems are as dangerous as ignoring failures entirely.
  • Set goals team by team with measurable specificity, and track them religiously. Uber's improvement machine works because every function — ads, fraud, conversion, courier batching — has its own numerical target. Continuous improvement requires specific accountability, not general exhortations.
  • Deliberately push risk-taking as companies get bigger, not less. Khosrowshahi explicitly pushes his leadership team to become more offensive as Uber grows — because larger companies can absorb more mistakes, not fewer. With $9.8B in free cash flow, some failed bets are survivable. Safe bets that miss the market are not.
  • When hiring someone you've gotten wrong twice, do the job yourself until you understand it. Khosrowshahi's rule: if he had missed a third hire for expedia.com's leadership role, the board should have fired him. So he ran the business himself for five years until he understood it well enough to hire correctly. This is how he learned to operate.
  • Test AI integration in your engineering team now and measure diffs. At Uber, 30% of engineers are already power users of AI coding tools, and they show measurable productivity gains in code output. Start measuring your own team's AI adoption and differential output today — the gap between power users and non-users will compound.
  • With your children and close relationships, have the actual deep conversations — do not defer them. Khosrowshahi's single biggest regret is the conversation with his father he never had because he assumed there would be more time. The regret is not abstract; he works to correct it daily in his relationships with colleagues and friends.
  • When evaluating major career moves, ask whether they create impact, not comfort. Daniel Ek's question — "Since when is life about being happy? It's about making impact" — prompted Khosrowshahi to call the Uber headhunter the next morning after saying no. Audit your current role against that standard.
